To clarify what I expect, here is an example of an analysis that would receive an “A.”

This is an example of an answer: This cartoon packs a lot of information and a biting
commentary into a small space. Titled “Blind Leading the Blind,” it caricatures the presidents
who led the United States into the war in Vietnam. At the beginning (left) is Eisenhower. This
misrepresents the origins of US involvement in Vietnam since it was Truman who began
supporting the French. To be historically accurate, the cartoon should begin with Truman
pushing Eisenhower, sporting sunglasses (he does not see the situation clearly) and holding a
golf club. (Ike played a lot of golf.) He is leaning toward his successor, Kennedy, and lightly
pushing him forward. This refers to the fact that Eisenhower’s policy of supporting Diem and
scuttling the elections in South Vietnam deepened US involvement there. Kennedy, also
sporting shades, carries a cane (he had a bad back and often used a cane), and his feet are
getting wet. He leans more heavily on Johnson than Eisenhower had leaned on him. This
signifies Kennedy’s escalation of US involvement in Vietnam, sending approximately 17,000
military advisers there, while using Agent Orange, napalm, and counterinsurgency tactics.
Johnson, still not seeing the situation clearly (wearing sunglasses), is ankle deep in the water
and shoves Nixon forward, which refers to his dramatic deepening of the war in Vietnam by
sending a half million US soldiers there. Nixon, although seeing the situation clearly, plunges
into the quagmire.
