Government and Medicine: The Two Horsemen of a Global Pandemic
For many, Covid began during the spring of 2020. Yet for the more attentive
ones, the people who kept a finger on the pulse of the world, reports of a deadly virus
originating in China had been circulating the globe since December. I remember hearing
about it heavily in January, but being the only person in my grade who voluntarily
listened to NPR on the way to school, my concerns were not held by my classmates. I
distinctly recall the beach vacation my family and friends took together where we
received the news that school would be delayed for two weeks. Everyone remembers
those two weeks; they were the beginning of the end, a naive embrace to this new “flu.”
The following months were full of regulation, bizarre global politics, rabid pushback to
masks and government mandates, a medical consensus as clear as mud, and enough
toilet paper shortages to make the Charmin toilet paper bears run and hide. While many
of the experiences we had during the COVID-19 pandemic mirrored those of the global
population during the 1918 pandemic, lack of medical equipment and increased
geopolitical stresses made the perfect substrate for the 1918 flu to wreak havoc across
the world, leaving a population scarred and crippled by the deadly virus.

keep their patients alive: “Treatment under direction of the physician is simple, but
important, consisting principally of rest in bed, fresh air, abundant food, with Dover’s
powder for relief of pain.” 5 The only real medicine in that plan is Dover’s powder, though
the efficacy of opium powder for a virus is questionable. Through both personal
experience and these sources, it is clear that for COVID-19, humans were well
equipped to battle the virus, armed to the teeth with fast vaccinations, DNA-sequencing,
ultrafine particle filters, and extreme cleaning products. On the other hand, the plan for
recovery in 1918 was little more than “sit back and pray,” allowing the flu to take every
victim it could.

Through readings of letters sent in and out of Camp Funston, the vagueness and lack of
importance in New York Times articles, and worldwide reporting of the flu, many would
conclude that information about the flu was a closely guarded secret. In a letter to Camp
Funston by Governor Arthur Capper, the governor asks the general to “wire or write me
as soon as possible a statement of actual conditions especially to the care that is being
given sick soldiers.” This message comes after a public outcry at the obscurity of the
care given to the soldiers. To understand why US soldiers were so heavily affected by
the flu, one must realize that the majority of the young men in the US were involved in
war efforts for WWI. Due to the US’s need to present a strong force to its allies, it was
business as normal for many, which led to severe outbreaks throughout military camps
where soldiers were housed in cramped barracks. During the COVID-19 pandemic,
countries were quick to openly communicate the status of their population, updating
infection and death numbers daily during the height of the virus. Instant communication

The final aspect of both the COVID-flu and the 1918 epidemic is more invisible.
In many reports, numbers focus on the dead, highlighting the effect the flu has on those
no longer living. This makes sense, as naturally we pay close attention to factors that
affect our survival. Yet something not mentioned often is the mental and physical effect
that something like a worldwide pandemic has on those who survive. For those of us
still living in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, the effect is obvious: an entire
generation of young students and children forced to live a significant portion of their
lives locked away, unable to connect with others. For many of my classmates, it stunted
their academic progress. For me, it forced my senior year to be one of social isolation,
meaningless take home exams, and non-existent sport’s seasons. For countless
people, it meant the death of immediate family, the vaporization of savings, and the
fragility of their economic situation as shops were shuttered, people were fired, and the
world went into survival mode. The 1918 pandemic was no different, with many first
hand accounts talking briefly about the mental toll it had on the survivors. In a memoir,
Paul Kendall talks about how his grandfather talked about his experience collecting

of us forget the harsh reality of the world closing down, the grimness of the pandemic
resurging only when we find a spare cloth mask tucked behind a car seat or into the
pockets of a winter coat seldom worn.

effects are still with us today, infused in the culture around the world. Personally, reading
letters, news articles, and memoirs about the 1918 flu was like being thrown back into
the middle of the pandemic, a blend of confusion, fear, and utter helplessness as we
watched the world around us suffer at the hands of the virus. The secretive, taboo
