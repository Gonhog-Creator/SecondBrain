I’ll be frank, Descartes wasn’t the brightest philosopher. Sure, he was the birthplace of “I
think therefore I am,” a rhetorical thought exercise known by millions, but the path through which he
arrives at this answer is convoluted and uninformed. In book two of Meditations, Descartes blathers
on extensively about a piece of wax, describing in intricate detail the wax and its physical properties
and then how the mind understands that when the wax melts, it is the same wax, despite the changes
in color or smell or shape. I will argue that had Descartesbeen taught rudimentary chemistry, he
would have been able to explain the transformation of the wax without defaulting to “the mind
alone,” and revealing it through “my imagination.” I would hope that a celebrated philosopher would
have better proof for something than the justification a child uses when arguing their imaginary
friend exists.
Descartes starts by noting all the physical features of the wax, a sign that by this point in his
meditations, the mind is trusting the senses (despite the deceiving evil demon.) He talks at length
about the many characteristics of the wax, saying “it has just been taken from the honeycomb; it has
not yet quite lost the taste of the honey; it retains some of the scent of the flowers from which it was
gathered; its color, shape and size are plain to see; it is hard, cold and can be handled without
difficulty; if you rap it with your knuckle it makes a sound.” It seems like Descartes got to know this
piece of wax quite well, though only by observing it. I would argue that instead of observing the
wax, understanding the reasons for its properties is vastly more important. If, as a child, Descartes
was taught simple molecular theory, he would understand that wax is a solid, made of long strands of
complex hydrocarbons that are bound together. Within the solid are aromatic hydrocarbons, leading
to the color and distinct smell, and that the flexible nature of these molecules allows it to be
transformed and molded into different shapes. Descartes needs to shift his source of understanding
from the physical senses to his mind.. If Descartes would peer into the wax and see not its smell nor

its color nor its taste, he would see with his mind a collection of molecules, differing in size from
large and small, each adding its characteristics to the wax as a whole. In addition, if he continued to
be taught about the chemistry of organic molecules that make up the lump of wax he seems so
focused on, he would be able to address the second conundrum he faces: the “immeasurable”
changes the wax can go through.
A stark moment of Descartes inability to think scientifically comes when he argues that the
mind cannot comprehend the complexity of the wax. He says “Not at all; for I can grasp that the
wax is capable of countless changes of this kind, yet I am unable to run through this immeasurable
number of changes in my imagination, from which it follows that it is not the faculty of imagination
that gives me my grasp of the wax as flexible and changeable.” Descartes is arguing that the
imagination is weak, and that it cannot be through imagination that we understand the wax as we
perceive it.
Descartes is true about one thing, the mind cannot comprehend “infinite”. If I handed you a
piece of cosmic energy and told you to explain the infinite ways it could change, our limited minds
would be overwhelmed with the possibilities. But for something as simple as wax, the possibilities
are whittled down to a reasonable amount, especially when taking into account the source of the
change – in this case, fire. We can understand through our mind that when a piece of wax is held to a
flame, the molecules within are heated, and in their state of high energy are able to move in such a
way that causes them to be a liquid. This is a change we can quantify and understand, and as such
melting points are a common statistic in determining different solids. Ice melts at a low temperature,
while iron melts at higher temperatures. The process is the same, and we understand the process by
which something melts. Despite this understanding, I already hear Descartes complaining, telling me
that while the mind can understand change in one tiny molecule of wax, the wax is too large to

comprehend all the changes at once. And to this I will tell him, “look towards the forest and tell me
what you see.” Unless he aims to be pedantic, he will respond with a description of the forest, and
how the leaves sway as the breeze travels through the canopy. It is here that I will explain how a leaf
grows, starting from a bud and unfurling over the days to a broad leaf, green and rugged. I will then
point to the countless leaves in the forest and ask “So how did all of those leaves grow?” Unless he
chooses to be ignorant, he will respond by saying “They each grew from a bud, unfurling over the
days.” It is in this way that we may understand the wax. For we can understand the simplicity of a
single molecule and its flexibility, and how another molecule gives the wax its color, and yet another
its smell. Once we understand the pieces, with our imagination and our mind we can collect them all
together and construct a final image, similar to how one sees a forest and does not think about the
roots underground or the leaves on the branches or the bark on the wood, although we can
understand each individually. Most people simply see a forest, and do not think twice.
Lastly we come to Descartes final conclusion, that “when I distinguish the wax from its
outward forms—take the clothes off, as it were, and consider it naked—then although my judgment
may still contain errors, at least my perception now requires a human mind.” While his method was
wrong, he came to the right conclusion. It is through the mind, and understanding each individual
part of the wax, that we are able to perceive the true nature of the wax and follow its changes until it
no longer becomes wax (such as when it burns and leaves nothing behind.) He was able to
understand that it was through the power of the mind that he could understand the wax past its
physical properties. If he had been taught basic molecular theory, he would have argued that it was
through the mind’s sense of understanding what cannot be seen that we extend the idea of the wax,
and therefore is “perceived by the mind alone.”
