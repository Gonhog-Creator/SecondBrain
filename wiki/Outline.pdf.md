The Advantages of Neutron Tomography for Non-Destructive Imaging of Lithium-Ion Batteries
The Nukes
Jose Barbeito, Kevin Huang, Grier Neese, Ryan Perry, Joshua Yagozinski

Abstract
This literature review focuses on neutron imaging techniques used to visualize the
physical structure of lithium ion batteries in operando. Lithium ion batteries are ubiquitous, used
to power many portable devices around the world such as phones, laptops, and headphones. The
batteries are also used in medical devices, drones, electric vehicles, and many other applications
where high electrical power density and rechargeability are required. Because lithium ion
batteries (LIBs) are so common, there is significant interest in improving their properties such as
power density, safety, cost, and reliability. However, to research these advancements, the internal
structure must be analyzed in a non destructive manner while the battery is in operation. The
parts of an LIB are degraded through charge cycling of the battery, resulting in problems such as
dendrite growth on the lithium anodes and gas generation due to decomposition of the electrolyte
and cathode. Neutron radiography has been proven to be an effective method of monitoring these
processes, as compared to conventional X-ray imaging techniques, neutrons are non destructive
and capable of achieving much higher contrast imaging of the battery, as unlike X-rays, neutrons
are easily attenuated by light elements such as lithium. While neutron imaging does not have as
high resolution as X-rays, it significantly increases the amount of information that can be
gathered from in operando studies of LIBs.

Motivations for Developing Neutron Imaging
● The development of neutron imaging has been motivated by the need to address
challenges in the industrialization of lithium metal electrodes with conventional liquid
electrolytes, specifically when understanding microscopic dendrite growth. Dendrite
growth, in the form of mosses, needles, and globules, is attributed to the leading cause of
short circuits and catastrophic failures in lithium batteries. [8] [7] [11]
● Gas generation during battery lifespan, caused by electrolyte decomposition and
structural release, poses performance and safety risks. Traditional imaging technologies
face limitations in observing gas evolution, making nondestructive probes like neutron
imaging with high-energy X-rays and neutrons essential for providing position
information on degradation. [6] [7] [8] [10]
● Usage of neutron imaging during battery discharge behavior reveals irreversible capacity
reduction, structural deterioration, and safety hazards in lithium-ion batteries. NI
techniques prove valuable in assessing lithium ion concentration, providing information
for safe battery design and estimating battery life. Alongside these assessments, NI shows
the importance in reducing the time consumed and the production costs of lithium ion
batteries. These studies open perspectives for battery characterization under real
operating conditions, especially for novel materials and designs. [6] [10] [11] [15]
● Operando neutron depth profiling (NDP) has been employed to investigate dendrite
formation mechanisms in solid electrolytes. Real-time visualization using NDP reveals
the direct deposit of lithium dendrites inside specific solid electrolytes, emphasizing the
importance of electronic conductivity in dendrite formation. [7] [19]
● Despite decades of theoretical modeling, experimental evidence suggests the complexity
of Li dendrite formation mechanisms extends beyond existing models. Advanced
materials characterization methods, particularly in situ and operando techniques like NI,
are essential for comprehensively understanding Li dendrite phenomena and overcoming
challenges posed by Li's reactivity and sensitivity.[1] [5] [15]

Advantages of Neutron Imaging
● Neutron imaging is crucial for gaining insights into lithium battery composition by
discerning between Li isotopes (6Li and 7Li). The unique ability of neutrons to distinguish
isotopes provides a better understanding of the source and structure of faults within the
electrode. Neutron imaging, as a non-invasive technique, offers high penetration depth,
making it the best option for studying lithium batteries during charging and discharging.
[1] [2] [4] [5] [6] [11] [16]

● In comparison to X-ray techniques, neutron imaging allows for non-destructive
characterization of battery interiors. Neutrons interact with atomic nuclei, not electrons,
making them highly penetrating and ideal for studying large objects like batteries. The
visibility of neutrons for light elements such as hydrogen and lithium enables direct
observation of phenomena like lithium diffusion, electrolyte consumption, and gas
formation. [1] [3] [6] [11]
● The isotopic sensitivity of neutron techniques allows for enormous contrast adaptability
and dynamic investigation of materials evolution, particularly in Li batteries. Neutron
imaging is particularly suitable for visualizing the 1-5 micron lithium dendrites, which
due to their small size have been difficult to analyze in the past. The technique's high
visibility for hydrogen and lithium makes it effective in tracking lithium exchange
processes within batteries. [3] [6] [11] [22]
● Non-destructive neutron-based methods, such as neutron radiographic imaging and
neutron depth profiling, offer valuable insights into electrochemical reactions, Li dendrite
formation, interfacial transport behavior, and the kinetics of Li transport in battery cells.
The high sensitivity of neutron imaging, compared to X-ray imaging, allows for the
investigation of Li plating behavior inside solid electrolytes and the visualization of Li
concentration profiling in battery cross-sections. [5] [12] [22]

Comparison to Similar Imaging Methods
● Neutron tomography is effective for in situ analysis and capturing the morphology of
Lithium deposits. Experiments have been completed that demonstrate the ability to
measure deposit volumes comparable to X-ray techniques. For higher resolution,
contrast, and shorter acquisition times there will need to be further optimizations.
Currently there are proposed improvements being made that include neutron-transparent
buffering of the metal layer and the ability to image a reduced cell diameter. [11]
● Neutron imaging and X-ray tomography reveal coral-like pore structures in lithiated
graphite electrodes through common orthogonal slices of their respective tomography.
Neutron imaging enhances contrast but has been found to not always lead to higher
resolution when images are reconstructed. [16] [22]
● Neutron imaging has advanced complementary to X-ray techniques, especially in battery
materials characterization. In X-ray synchrotron applications beam damage can be
problematic whereas neutron imaging does not cause any measurable radiation damage.
Neutron-induced sample activation may pose logistical challenges but is usually
short-lived. [21]
● Neutron radiograms and X-ray tomography both highlight higher neutron absorption in
polymeric foams. Enhanced contrast in neutron imaging does not necessarily guarantee
higher resolution in reconstructed tomography. [16]
● In-situ TEM is a major breakthrough in battery research and had proven reliable for
studying Lithium dendrites due to high spatial resolution and lower beam damage.
Utilizing for dendrites does have disadvantages which can lead to deviations from
commercial cell conditions and potentially invalid conclusions. [5]

Synthesis with Other Methods
● Currently electron and X-ray techniques rely on the atomic number of the atom for
contrast but have difficulty with heavier transition metal elements. Neutron scattering
addresses these limitations and is helping to fill the gaps in accurately describing the
structure’s response to Lithium batteries.[2]
● Combining X-ray and neutron imaging provides valuable benefits for interpreting
materials with different attenuation properties. This provides a versatile approach and is
beneficial for optimizing various battery chemistries and applicable rechargeable cells. [20]
● Nondestructive neutron imaging compensates for X-ray deficiencies in analyzing Lithium
metal. This imaging allows the observation of Lithium electrodeposits which enables
monitoring of the metal dendrite growth and volume changes. [6]
● Custom built Li-ion batteries offer a thorough analysis using diverse techniques as well
as a higher resolution in neutron powder diffraction when synchronized with X-ray
diffraction radiography. [15]

Restrictions/Limitations of Neutron Imaging
● Due to increased difficulty of detecting neutrons vs more conventional methods, getting
the resolution required for some analysis can be lengthy or costly. [11] [21]
● Due to the nature of neutrons, generating them in a laboratory setting is more expensive
than other methods such x-rays [5]
● “Although, NPD is robust technique to depth profiling of Li concentration, it does not
allow for direct visualization of Li metal microstructures.” [5]
