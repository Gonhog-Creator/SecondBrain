Problem 1 (45 points total)
Here is a problem based on experimental data from the Genzer research group. A polymer coating
can be generated by grafting one end of polymer chains to a solid support (typically glass-like
substrate). The systems are characterized by the length of the polymer chains (i.e., their molecular
weight) and the density of the grafts on the substrate (so-called grafting density, s, defined as the
number of chains per unit area of the substrate surface). Due to various factors (both reaction
related as well as crowding of chains as they grow away from the substrate), the length of the
polymer grafts may vary considerably (see left cartoon in Figure 1). We can measure the distribution of the molecular weights by using a technique called size exclusion chromatography (SEC).
However, we need to first remove the polymers from the substrate before we pass them through
the SEC apparatus (see more details in References 1,2, 3 and 4). A chemical called tetrabutyl
ammonium fluoride (TBAF) is used to selectively cleave Si-O bonds, which attach the polymers
to the substrate. We monitor the progress of degrafting over time and obtain values of the grafting
density of the remaining polymers at each instant (in Figure 1, the degrafted chains are marked in
light blue and the chains remaining on the surface are in dark blue).

Figure 1. Schematic depicting degrafting of macromolecules from solid substrates. The grafting density of polymer
grafts on the surface (s) decreases with increasing time.
