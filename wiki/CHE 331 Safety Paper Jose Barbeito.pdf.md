Jose Barbeito
11/21/24
CHE331 Safety Paper
Caught in the Storm: Extreme Weather Hazards
This video by the USCSB covers the incident at the Arkema Chemical Plant that occurred in
2017 during and after the landfall of Hurricane Harvey in Crosby Texas. The video dives deep
into what occurred during the Hurricane, highlighting the bravery of the hurricane crew, the
faulty actions of the first responders, and the lack of guidelines and preparations by forces larger
than Arkema.
In 2017, Hurricane Harvey made landfall in Texas. Prior to the first rain, the entire plant,
which produces organic peroxides, had been shut down and prepared. All peroxides were kept in
warehouses and trailers, which were cooled below 32 degrees to prevent the peroxide from
decomposing and catching fire, the major risk factor in the product. As the first rain began to fall,
the only people at the plant were the “ride out” crew, which were there to oversee the plant
during the hurricane. Their actions would significantly alter the course of events over the next 72
hours.
As the rain fell, the water level rose. Since its construction in 1960, the plant had been
considered above the flood zone. Only in 2007 did new information reveal that they were not
outside the flood zone, almost 50 years after the construction of the plant. Because of this and the
reports from local authorities, the ride out crew believed that the water levels would stop rising
and that there was no further risk to the product or buildings. Despite their beliefs, the water
levels continued to rise. At a certain level, the electrical systems of several warehouses were at
risk of flooding, and so the ride out crew moved the product out of those warehouses and to
another, which sat on higher ground, shutting off the power to those buildings. Still, the rain kept
falling. Eventually, the water levels rose too much, causing the main transformers to flood and
stop functioning. The backup generators, which sat at a higher elevation, kicked on, cooling the
last warehouse and attached cooling trailers. Fearing that the flood waters would reach the
backup generators, the ride out crew moved the remaining product to cooling trailers parked on
higher ground, just in time for the backup generators to stop working. During this movement, the
truck used to move the trailers flooded, leaving three cooling trailers still attached to the
warehouse. As there was still product in the warehouse, workers moved peroxide via forklift to
the attached trailers. When the forklift flooded as well, the workers moved the last 2,000
containers by hand through the floodwaters. Despite this, the water kept rising, causing the
remaining trailers to fail. The peroxides were no longer cooled, and began to decompose.
The ride out crew was evacuated (in addition to a 1.5 mile radius around the plant, which
included around 200 people), and several hours later several of the trailers caught fire, releasing
toxic gasses to the surrounding area. Police, traveling near the highway, were exposed to the
fumes and reported a white smoke. After this initial report, the highway was closed. Yet after
being checked several hours later, there was no smoke detected, and the highway was reopened
due to its importance in evacuation procedures for the surrounding area. Smoke was detected
again on the highway, causing several people to seek medical attention. No deaths were reported.
This incident was, I believe, to be an extreme edge case. Hurricane Harvey delivered so
much water it was deemed a “greater than 500 year storm.” The ride out crew was valiant in their
efforts to maintain the product in cooled locations, moving product through the night and even

by hand for hours to keep the peroxides from decomposing. There were no operational issues
that caused this accident, nor were there maintenance issues that could have been prevented. In
fact, there were a large number of backup and failure systems in place, but none of the equipment
was prepared for such a high level of water. The biggest thing that could have been done for
prevention was accurate analysis of the flood zone and preparation. Even though the plant was
deemed to be in a flood zone in 2007, the insurance company and other regulatory bodies had no
recommendations for the plant, deeming it “safe” from floods. Clearly, this was not true. Not
only were there no recommendations, there were no overarching guidelines for chemical plants
of this type for preparation, action, or planning for a 500 year storm such as hurricane Harvey.
The biggest takeaway is that companies and regulators need to be extra safe and have a
conservative approach when evaluating risk, including ensuring that there is no single failure
mode for all systems (though I would argue it is hard for a system to not be susceptible to
damage when being completely underwater).
The video had excellent quality, though the repetition of simulated clips was a bit boring.
I rate it a 7/10. The effectiveness of the video I rate a 5/10, as I would say the biggest take away
from the video, which was mirrored in the Youtube comments, was the bravery and effort of the
ride out crew, who did everything in their power to keep the product from combusting. While it
calls on regulatory bodies and plant engineers to be more conservative in their flooding
estimates, many engineers who are making financial decisions would consider building for a 500
year flood when the plant may only be in operation for 10, 50, or 75 years is a fool’s errand. I
still believe the message to be important, hence the higher score.
For a safety exercise, I would love to suggest something similar to Dr. Cooper’s senior
design safety video game. I recommend (as I would find it fun and engaging), a type of Sim-City
type game, where you physically design a plant (simple layout, with drag and drop building
blocks), and oversee the operation over the course of 10, maybe 50 years, speeding up time to
quickly pass through the years. Every once in a while, an extreme event would happen, such as
the damage of equipment due to not practicing maintenance checks, or major weather events like
hurricanes or extreme cold. This would give you a real idea of how often these things are, how to
plan/prepare for them, and their consequences. You could grade based on the number of failures,
or on how well the plant survived, or simply on a reflection type paper such as this one. I believe
it would be very beneficial. Thank you for a wonderful semester!
