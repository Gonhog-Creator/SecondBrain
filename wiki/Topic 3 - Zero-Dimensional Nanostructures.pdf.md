Quantum Confinement
When the size of a nanocrystal approaches the de
Broglie wavelength of electrons (wave-particle duality)
in the conduction band, e- and h+ are spatially
confined, dipoles are formed and discrete electronic
energy levels arise, and shift Eg (band gap). This is
known as quantum confinement
This is most pronounced for semiconductor NPs (2-10
nm), but also observed in metals (<2 nm) and
insulators

Desirable Nanoparticle Characteristics
Small size is not the only requirement. For
any practical application, synthesis & stability
needs to be controlled in such a way that the
resulting NPs have the following
characteristics:
Identical and stable properties, which
requires:
1. Identical size of all particles, aka
monosized or uniform size distribution
2. Identical shape or morphology
3. Identical chemical composition
4. Identical crystal structure
5. Core and surface compositions must be
the same
6. Individually dispersed or monodispersed,
i.e., no agglomeration
7. If agglomeration occurs, particles must
be readily redispersible

Homogeneous Nucleation
NPs can be synthesized through homogeneous
nucleation in three media: liquid, gas and solid.
The fundamentals of nucleation and subsequent
growth are the same, so we will focus on solutions.
Nucleation requires supersaturation of growth
species, i.e., the concentration of solute to exceed
the equilibrium solubility (or temperature to
decrease below the phase transformation point),
for example:

Melting pt

– Reduction of temperature in most systems reduces
solubility and can cause supersaturation;
Note: some materials exhibit an inverse temperature
solubility behavior
– In situ chemical reactions can transform precursors and
change their solubility, leading to supersaturation
– Solvent can evaporate, leading to supersaturation, but
this approach is not used for controlled NP nucleation
and growth

Nanoparticle Size Analysis
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)
– Direct
– High resolution ~0.1 nm (size and shape)
– Technically demanding sample preparation and high cost

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)
– Direct
– Resolution limited to >1 nm (size and shape)
– Requires conducting samples

Scanning probe microscopy (SPM), e.g., AFM, STM
– Direct
– High resolution in z (<0.1 nm), low resolution in x-y
– Tip effects, and often sample-independent

X-ray diffraction (XRD)
– Indirect
– Not suitable for polycrystalline, core-shell, or noncrystalline NPs, or anisotropic samples

Dynamic light scattering (DLS)
– Indirect
– Medium resolution (3 nm) (size only, no shape)
