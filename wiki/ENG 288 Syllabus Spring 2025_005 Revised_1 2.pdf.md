Course Description:
Welcome to Introduction to Fiction Writing! The goal of this course is to expose students to all of the
different voices, subjects, and forms that fiction can take and to provide tools, strategies, and innovative
language to craft the genre, question its boundaries, and assist students in finding their unique authorial
voice. Students will learn to write about an issue or event in a personal, stylish way while maintaining
rigorous and scrupulous artistic integrity. Students will engage in writing in a collaborative manner,
workshopping, editing, and revising their pieces. In sum, we will read stories, we will read essays about
writing stories, we will write stories, and we will do it altogether. My best advice for you in this class is to
put in what you want out of the course and write what you truly want to write.
Course Objectives/Outcomes:
●​ Students will read and critically engage with fiction texts representing a diverse range of topics,
subgenres, and perspectives.
●​ Students will learn about, and put into practice, conventions and characteristics of creative fiction.
●​ Students will compose a series of fiction texts, and take them through the processes of idea
formulation, drafting, workshopping, and revision.
●​ Students will engage in a collaborative writing process.
●​ Students will develop an awareness of audience and work to construct an ethos and voice that
responds to audience feedback.
●​ Students will experiment with voice, styles, and forms.
●​ Students will learn to take responsibility for their own learning, their commitment to their craft,
and their formulation of an empathetic, critically-minded learning community.

GRADING:
How to get a “B” Grade in this course:
1.​ Presence and Participation: You fully participate in at least 85% (23/27) of our scheduled
class sessions and their activities and assignments, which means you will need to be present in
class. We will all get the most out of this class when we are all present (not online shopping or
checking iMessage), taking risks, sharing ideas, and participating in discussion. I understand not
everyone “participates fully” in the same way. Some of you may say five auditory words over the
course of the semester and prefer to listen & reflect through writing; some of you will lead
discussion so often you may be asked to step back and invite others in. Written feedback for your
peers and Moodle discussion forums are also included in my definition of “full participation.” But
(and it’s a big but) you cannot miss more than 4 class sessions and still receive a “B.”
**If I mark you absent/non-participatory, I’ll email you to inform you & check in on you.
NOTE: Assignments not turned in because of an absence, either ones assigned on the schedule or
ones assigned on earlier days in class, will be late, missed, or ignored (depending on when you
turn it in)(see the guidelines below).
Any EXCUSED absence due to a university-sponsored group activity (e.g., sporting event, band,
another class field trip, etc.), military-related absences (e.g. deployment, work, duty, etc.), jury
duty, or documented illness will be considered independently of the above attendance policy, as
long as the student has FIRST provided written documentation (email Shelby!!) as soon as
they are aware of the days they will be absent. This may include absences due to illness that
has a medical/doctor’s note confirming the illness. Each of these circumstances will be
determined on a case-by-case basis in consultation between the student and instructor in a manner
that is fair to all parties involved. See NC State’s official absence policy here:
http://policies.ncsu.edu/regulation/reg-02-20-03.
2.​ Completed Work: You turn in all work and assignments expected of you in the spirit they are
assigned and on time. This completed work includes:
●​ Final Portfolio submitted ON TIME. Must include at least 20 total pages
(double-spaced, 12 pt Times New Roman font).
●​ Workshop in-line comments and workshop letters for each member of your workshop
group
●​ Workshop manuscripts submitted ON TIME
●​ Midterm Writing Reflection (in-class)
●​ Assigned Moodle Discussion Forums (questions for visiting writers must be
submitted ON TIME)
●​ Come Find My Desk! assignment (due at the end of the semester ON TIME)

3.​ Late Work: During the semester, you may turn in assignments late. You are adults, and you
know how to manage your time better than I know how to manage your time. However, I cannot
review a million late assignments at the end of the semester, and I want to ensure that there is
some accountability for folks to fully participate in classroom discussion. So, late assignments
MUST be turned in within 48 hours of the deadline for the assignment. At the bottom of this
grading contract section is a table which stipulates the number of late assignments allowed for
each letter grade.
NOTE: Workshop manuscripts CANNOT be submitted late. If you do not show up for your
assigned workshop time slot, you will forfeit your workshop slot and receive an automatic
“ignored” work for the assignment. Missed workshops cannot be made up, but if you have a
scheduling conflict for an EXCUSED absence or you are ill, please email me IMMEDIATELY
and we can reschedule you. Furthermore, final portfolios, questions for visiting writers, and
the Come Find My Desk! assignment MUST be submitted on time. Please be considerate of
our guests and of my time and energy at the end of the semester. Last minute technology
malfunctions will not be accepted as an excuse for late work.
4.​ Forgotten Work: If you turn in late work AFTER the 48 hours stipulated above
(Late/Incomplete Work), then it will be considered “forgotten work,” which is a more serious
mark against your final grade. This is due to the fact that all assignments are used in class when
they are due, so turning in something beyond 48 hours after it is due means it is assured to be less
useful, and its absence has hurt your colleagues in class (since they depended on you to complete
your work for the benefit of the entire classroom environment).
5.​ Ignored Work: You do not ignore any work expected of you. Ignored work is any work
unaccounted for in the semester—that is, I have no record of you doing it or turning it in. I
believe that ignoring work so crucial to your development as a learner in our classroom hurts the
learning of others, which is unacceptable.
The grades of A (4.0) or A+ (4.33) require more labor that helps or supports the class in its mutual aims.
In order to raise your grade, you may complete as many of the following items as you like. Each item
completed fully and in the appropriate manner will raise your final course grade by one grade
category.
●​ Final Reflection essay: In addition to your final portfolio, you may submit a final reflection
essay (1000+ words). This final reflection essay is not a full-blown academic essay but should
reference at least four readings from the semester’s assignments and should attend to some or
all of the following questions: What have you learned about writing this semester? What have
you learned about yourself as a writer this semester? Which aspects of this course were the most
challenging? Which aspects of this course were the most (un)helpful? How does your final
portfolio demonstrate what you have learned from this course? What are you proud of this
semester? How will you continue to practice what you’ve learned in this course?
●​ Attending two literary events on campus: Over the course of the semester, the English
department, other NC State departments, nearby universities, and local bookstores will host
readings by writers. I will keep you posted on these events so that you can attend. Attendance at
these events is highly encouraged—even just for fun! To go beyond the default grade, please
attend two events and draft a short (250 word), reflective write-up about each event.

●​ Assist with a lesson (TedTalk): You can create a 20 minute presentation or lesson plan that
pertains to this class. Take a look at the Semester Schedule. Are there any topics or readings that
stand out to you? Something you think is missing? If you would like to assist with a lesson,
please email me (Shelby) at least two weeks in advance. For your presentation, you will need to
submit a brief lesson plan/outline to me as well as a short reflective essay (250 words)
post-presentation about what you learned from preparing and teaching your lesson.
●​ Some other labor that benefits the class and our mutual learning. Do you want to write about
and report to us on a cultural event related to the class? Or produce a podcast? Or maybe you
would like to read an article for us and summarize some of its findings or ideas that you think will
help us do our work in class? If you have an idea, come to me (Shelby) early in the semester. I
will work with you to plan your endeavor and make sure the amount of labor is commensurate
and equitable with the above items.
For every item you complete on the above list, your grade will improve by one grade category. So if
you meet all the conditions for a “B grade” (3.0), then your grade can improve in the following ways:
●​ 1 item completed = course grade of 4.0 (A)
●​ 2 items completed = course grade of 4.33 (A+)
●​ 3 items completed = you’re just an awesome human!
If you only meet the requirements for a “C grade” (2.0) or lower, the same movement up the grade ladder
applies by completing items on the list above. You may even do more than two items and continue to raise
your grade regardless of what grade you are qualified for. For example, if you only meet the requirements
for a “C grade” (2.0) in the table below, but do 3 extra labors above (the max), then your final course
grade will be an A+ (4.33).

Workshops: Writing is about community and communication thus feedback is vital for writers to
improve their craft. A writing workshop is a supportive space where writers work together to enhance
each other's work. As your instructor, I will work to provide the foundation for a productive,
collaborative, and empathetic workshop space. Criticism is part of writing and reading, but bullying or
meanness of any kind will not be tolerated.
Due to the size of our class, we will only be able to conduct a full class workshop on one of your pieces
at the end of the semester. Other workshops will be conducted in small groups. For every workshop, you
will be required to submit your drafts in advance for your peers to be able to read and engage with your
work prior to the date of your workshop. You will also be required to make in-line comments on each
writer’s submitted piece and write short feedback letters (250-500 words) for the first two
workshops. These document copies and feedback letters should be uploaded to Google Drive before the
writer’s workshop. More direction will be provided before workshop. These feedback letters should
answer the following questions: What is working well? What needs more improvement? What
questions, suggestions, or What Ifs do you have?

Academic integrity: Regardless of discipline, honest and rigorous scholarship is at the foundation of a
Research I institution. Students are bound by the academic integrity policy as stated in NCSU Code of
Student Conduct: http://policies.ncsu.edu/policy/pol-11-35-01. Students are required to uphold the
university pledge of honor and exercise honesty in completing every assignment. Plagiarism is the act of
taking someone or something else’s ideas and passing it off as your own—this includes using Chat GPT
to generate/complete assignments. Plagiarism will not be tolerated. Violations of academic integrity will
result in referral to the Office of Student Conduct with a recommendation of a failing grade for the
assignment, and they will be reported to the department head.
