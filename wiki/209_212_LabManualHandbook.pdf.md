Teaching Reasoning through Introductory Physics Lab Experiments

Introduction
There is a lot more to physics than concepts and equations. This lab course is designed to help you learn
how to develop a reliable understanding of the physical world, how to devise and test scientific knowledge
claims, and how to communicate scientific ideas with other people. The lab course is based on three
main ideas:
1. Making and comparing measurements (labs 1-3 and 10-11)
2. Using graphs to find mathematical relationships (labs 4-6 and 12-13)
3. Building models of complex phenomena (labs 7-9 and 14-17)
In this lab course, you will learn essential skills and ideas in Pre-Lab Readings. Each pre-lab reading is
followed by a Pre-Lab Quiz, which you must complete before the start of your lab period. During the lab,
you will practice those skills while conducting experimental work by following the Instructions. Together
with your lab partner(s), you will submit a Lab Report at the end of your lab period. The lab report consists
of 2 items that you will need to submit for each lab, such as a paragraph-length answers to questions,
descriptions of your experimental procedure, graphs, data, or photos.
Every few labs, you will conduct an Investigation. Investigations are more open-ended than labs, giving
you a chance to devise your own experimental procedure and demonstrate your new skills. For
investigations, your lab report needs to be more comprehensive, typically including a description of your
procedure, data, a conclusion, and other elements.
During your experimental work, you will be supported by an instructor. Your instructor will deliver a brief
introductory lecture at the start of your lab period. So don't be late! At two points during each lab (but not
investigations), you will have a conversation with your instructor about your progress. This is called a
Checkpoint. In addition, please feel free to ask your instructor for help or advice at any time as you work
through this lab course.
This lab course was written following best practices (Kozminski et al, 2017) for introductory experimental
physics. One part of that is making lab courses accessible (Dounas-Frazer et al, 2021). While this lab
course was designed to be as universally accessible as possible, there will still be barriers for some
students. For that reason, each lab includes an Accessibility Notes section with advice and resources for
students who may require additional layers of accessibility.
Since these labs are about experimental physics, you will not need to know any physics concepts in order
to complete your lab reports. In a few cases where they are needed, physics concepts are introduced in
the pre-lab readings. Thus, the labs will not be aligned with your physics lectures. However, please feel
free to use any physics concepts that you think may be relevant, if that helps you complete your lab-work.
Your work will be graded using a 5-point rubric. In brief, expect to receive 3 points if your work has major
issues, 4 points if your work is mostly correct but has one or two shortcomings (missing units, incorrect
formatting, etc), and 5 points if your work is complete and correct.

v. Dealing with outliers. In scientific data, you may occasionally encounter outliers, which are data points
that do not fit the expected trend. In this experiment, a fall time might be an outlier if the helicopter hits
the table while it falls, if the helicopter flips over because of a bad drop, or any of a number of other
reasons. When dealing with outliers, it is important to distinguish between measurement outliers and post
hoc outliers:
-

-

A measurement outlier occurs when the measurement system has some sort of error or
malfunction, resulting in the collection of incorrect data. If the experimenter is paying close
attention, they can spot a system outlier while they are collecting the data.
A post hoc outlier occurs when the data is collected and analyzed, and only at that time does it
become apparent that a data point doesn't follow the expected trend. Post hoc outliers are not
obviously caused by errors or malfunctions in the experimental system.

It is important when collecting data to note measurement outliers when they occur, and to exclude them
from the data set. On the other hand, it is important that you do not remove post hoc outliers from your
data, as this could be considered a form of scientific malpractice. Pause for a minute and discuss with
your partner: what is the difference between a measurement outlier and a post hoc outlier? How should
you address outliers in this lab, and in future physics labs?

In experimental science, we use the term "error" differently from how it is used in ordinary conversation,
where it means something like "mistake". Instead, in experimental science, "error" means that inevitable
uncertainty that comes from all measurements. In this sense, errors are not mistakes, and you cannot
entirely avoid them by being careful. Thus, there is no such thing as "human error": in the lab, if you make
a mistake, you should repeat the measurement. While conducting an experiment, we should (a) try to
make the error as small as possible, and (b) try to estimate the size of the error so we can know how
reliable our measurements are.
For example, think about a student who is measuring the fall time for a paper helicopter. For the first drop,
the student measures a time of 5.2 s. For the second drop, the student measures a time of 4.7 s. For the
third drop, the student was distracted and pressed the start button at the wrong time, resulting in a time
measurement of 1.2 s. The third measurement is a mistake (not an error!), resulting in a measurement
outlier, and this data point should be excluded from further analysis. However, the fact that the first two
measurements (5.2 s and 4.7 s) are not exactly the same tells that there is some error in these
measurements.
The simplest way to determine the error associated with a measured value is to make many
measurements and report the range of those measurements. For example, in the graph shown below, the
fall time for Helicopter 1 has a range of 6.7 to 11.1 s.

However, you might be suspicious of this approach. If you collect more data, you'll expect most of the
data points to cluster around the average value, but you'll get a few data points that are further away just
because of random effects. These random effects are not outliers. Rather, they reflect that there is
inherent uncertainty in making any measurement. There is a very nice mathematical idea called the
Central Limit Theorem that predicts that the distribution of a large number of measurements will take on
the shape of a normal distribution (also called a bell curve). In other words, imagine you were to make

The normal distribution curve depends on only two parameters. The first parameter is the mean, which

the standard deviation, which tells us about the width of the distribution, and is represented by the greek

As the image below shows, we expect that about 68.2% of the measurements should have values within 1

11.

So, what does this mean for us, as experimental scientists? If we take many repeated measurements, we
can estimate the mean from the normal distribution by calculating the average value of our
measurements. Likewise, we can calculate the standard deviation (SD) of those measurements. While
you could do these calculations by hand, it is much easier to use a spreadsheet such as Microsoft Excel
or Google Sheets. Here's an example:

The equation in cell I2 that calculates the average is
=AVERAGE(a2:h2)
and the equation in cell J2 that calculates the standard deviation is
=STDEV(a2:h2)
These equations work the same in Excel or Sheets. Therefore, we have a good way to calculate the range
of values that we could record for any given measurement. From the data on the above spreadsheet, we
could say that, on average, about 68% of our measurements should be between 4.7 and 5.3.
However, that's not exactly what we mean by error, which is how certain we are about our measurement.
Imagine that we take 8 measurements and find that the average time is 5 seconds. If we then go back and
take 100 more measurements, and the average is still 5 seconds, we will be more certain that 5 seconds
is a reliable measurement for the time. However, the standard deviation doesn't capture this. Instead, we
need to use the standard error (SE), which is given by

Each of our five mathematical relationships has "free" parameters (𝐴, 𝐵, 𝐶) which are determined by
fitting the best-fit line to the data. However, if we don't have very many data points, this fitting process
doesn't capture the pattern of the data: it's just a game of connect-the-dots.
Imagine applying a linear best-fit line to two data points:

As you can see on the graph, a linear best-fit line (which has two parameters, 𝐴 and 𝐵) simply connects
the two dots. This is because you can always draw a straight line that connects two points. In other
words, there isn't any freedom for the best-fit line to be drawn anywhere except through the two points.

So, the RMSE tells us how far away each measured data point is from the best-fit line. On a graph, you
could find the RMSE by first finding all the vertical distances (indicated below in blue) between the data
points and the best-fit line, squaring each distance, adding them all together, and then taking the square
root of that number.

In practice, since the RMSE tells you something about how far away the data points are from the model, it
can be useful if you are trying to figure out which model fits the data best. If you have two models, the
model with the smaller RMSE will tend to be the model that fits the data best. For example, of the two
graphs showing nighttime noise levels, the linear model has an RMSE of 2.91, whereas the square root
model has an RMSE of 2.14. Therefore, we could conclude that the square root model is more likely to be
the correct relationship between average noise level and population density.

In the previous lab, we introduced the idea of modeling, the process of developing and using
models to understand phenomena. When engaging in modeling in an experimental physics lab,
we typically use two models: the measurement model, and the conceptual model. The
measurement model has to do with how we make measurements, and the conceptual model
relates to the physics ideas we are using, often through the use of multiple representations.
This week, we will delve into the modeling process a little deeper, using a framework developed
by Zwickl et al. (2014) and refined by Dounas-Frazer et al. (2018). The framework, illustrated
below, helps us understand how the measurement model and conceptual model are used when
we do experimental science work.

Here's an example: You might develop your understanding of a system by describing it via
multiple representations. This can lead you to make predictions, or hypotheses, about how the
system would respond as you make changes. Meanwhile, you can work with the actual system
to figure out how to make accurate, precise measurements. Once you've collected your

experimental results, you can perform a comparison between the experimental results and your
predictions. If your experimental results and your predictions don't agree, you may need to work
to improve your theory. If your experimental results and your predictions do agree, you might
need to improve your measurement process in order to make your error bars smaller.
For example, consider the discovery of the Higgs Boson in 2012. Experimental physicists built a

Lab 9 - Greenhouse Effect - Pre-Lab Reading
When light hits the surface of a non-transparent material, some of the incident light will be absorbed, and
the rest of the light will be reflected. The fraction of the light energy that is reflected is called the albedo,
and represented with the greek letter alpha (α).
𝐸𝑟𝑒𝑓𝑙𝑒𝑐𝑡𝑒𝑑 = α 𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑐𝑖𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑡
Therefore, the fraction of the light that will be absorbed will be proportional to (1 − α).
𝐸𝑎𝑏𝑠𝑜𝑟𝑏𝑒𝑑 = (1 − α) 𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑐𝑖𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑡
The light energy that gets absorbed is transformed into thermal energy. Thus, the temperature of the
absorbing material will increase when light shines on it. By measuring the increase in temperature for
different substances, you can infer the albedo of those substances.
Likewise, light may also travel through a material if that material is partially transparent. Absorbance is the
fraction of light that is absorbed by light passing through a material, and is represented by a capital A.
𝐸𝑎𝑏𝑠𝑜𝑟𝑏𝑒𝑑 = 𝐴 𝐸𝑖𝑛𝑐𝑖𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑡
For example, if a lamp emits 100 J of energy that passes through a tank of water with an absorbance of
0.3, then 30 J of energy will be absorbed by the water and 70 J of energy will pass through the water.
Likewise, if a lamp emits 100 J of energy that shines on a strip of asphalt with albedo 0.08, then 8 J of
energy will be reflected from the asphalt and 92 J of energy will be absorbed by the asphalt.

Each lab, you will need to complete the pre-lab reading assignment and then answer the

In this lab, and for several subsequent labs, we will be using a framework for understanding experimental
science called Modeling. Based on the work of Zwickl et al. (2014) and Dounas-Frazer et al. (2018), there
are two pieces involved in the process of modeling.
When dealing with complex phenomena with many different variables, scientists work to develop an
understanding of the phenomenon by building a conceptual model. Good conceptual models can be
represented in several different ways, allowing scientists to use diagrams, equations, words, numbers, or
graphs as needed. For example, when dealing with circuits, you might draw a circuit diagram, write an
equation to represent the electric potential differences in the circuit, collect measurements in a table, or
make a graph of two variables in the circuit.
In order to connect the conceptual model and the real world, scientists also develop a measurement
model to represent the apparatus and procedure they use for making measurements. The measurement
model accounts for the assumptions we make as we conduct experiments and collect and analyze data.

As an example of how the modeling process works, think about efforts to understand climate change.
Scientists have developed conceptual models that aim to account for radiation from the sun, the amount
of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, the amount of clouds, and many other factors. These conceptual
models were originally simple enough that they could be calculated by hand, but now they are complex
and sophisticated enough that they require supercomputers in order to generate predictions for the
future. At the same time, other scientists are continuing to develop increasingly sensitive and reliable
ways to measure current global temperatures, cloud cover, and carbon dioxide levels using satellites;
investigate historical trends by looking at ice cores from Antarctica; and account for complex factors like
heat islands and the release of methane from warming permafrost. Warnings about the effects of
anthropogenic global warming come when the conceptual and measurement models are combined,
providing incredibly powerful and reliable accounting for past climate trends and predictions for the
future.

In the previous lab, you were introduced to the modeling framework for experiment science developed by
Zwickl et al. (2014) and refined by Dounas-Frazer et al. (2018). This framework has two parts: a
conceptual model that uses multiple representations to explain a phenomenon with scientific theory, and
a measurement model that focuses on the use of measurements to collect relevant data. A more
complete sketch of the model is illustrated below:

According to this more-complete sketch, the goal of modeling is to use the conceptual model to make
testable predictions about the world, and to use the measurement model to collect experimental results.
Then, after comparing the predictions and the experimental results, we are provided some feedback. If the
results and predictions agree, this tells us that the two models are well-aligned, and we should improve
our measurements in order to make the error bars smaller and seek discrepancies between the
conceptual model and the measurement model. On the other hand, if the results and predictions disagree,
that tells us that we should go back to our conceptual model and work to improve our theoretical
understanding.
In this lab, we will employ this framework in order to develop conceptual and measurement models for a
light-emitting diode (LED). This lab is inspired by a paper by Etkina and Planinšič (2014).
An LED is made of two types of semiconductors, a p-type and an n-type. When the negative side of a
battery is connected to the n-type semiconductor, an abundance of free electrons are able to flow through
the n-type semiconductor. When the positive side of a battery is connected to the p-type superconductor,
electrons are removed from the semiconductor, leaving electron "holes". If the two semiconductors are in
close proximity, the electrons can jump from the n-type semiconductor to the p-type semiconductor, filling

suitable materials for the n-type semiconductor, the p-type semiconductor, and the protective coating for

conducting investigations. In this lab, you will use the modeling framework to develop an understanding

This lab is about magnets and magnetic forces. In order to get started, let's begin with a force we know
about: gravity. It is a force that acts on masses, and it follows an "inverse-square" law:
2

𝐹𝑔𝑟𝑎𝑣 = 𝐺 𝑀 𝑚 / 𝑟

The electric force is also an inverse-square law, and it acts on objects that have electric charge.
2

𝐹𝑒𝑙𝑒𝑐 = 𝑘 𝑄 𝑞 / 𝑟

While there is only one type of mass (things either have mass, or they don't), there are two types of
electric charge: positive, and negative. The electric force between two positive charges is repulsive, and it
is attractive between a positive charge and a negative charge. You can account for this by treating the

The magnetic force is a bit more complicated. You could write down a force law for the magnetic force,
like the ones for the gravitational and electric force above, but it wouldn't tell you anything useful. One
issue is that there are no magnetic "charges" in the universe. These would be called magnetic monopoles,
and have been the subject of an intense, but fruitless, decades-long search. Magnets appear only as
dipoles, with two opposite charges that we call the North and South sides of the magnet.

●

ferromagnetic material is iron.
Other types of materials, such as diamagnetic materials and paramagnetic materials interact with

Lab 17 - Light - Pre-Lab Reading
Previously, you have seen and worked with the modeling framework for experimental science. As you
might recall, the framework includes two components: a measurement model that focuses on making
reliable and accurate measurements and generating experimental results, and a conceptual model that
uses multiple representations to use scientific theory in order to make predictions about the world.
Through the process of comparing experimental results with predictions, we are able to improve either or
both of these models by refining our measuring process or revisiting the scientific theory.

In this week's lab, you will investigate the interaction of light with transparent materials (glass or acrylic)
as you develop effective and robust measurement and conceptual models. As you do this, you are
following in the footsteps of many great scientists from around the world. In order to illustrate how the
modeling process works in practice, here's a bit of the story about one phenomena you will be studying in
this lab: refraction.
In ancient China, around 2000 years ago, tradespeople developed a sophisticated understanding of how
to create and fire ceramics in high-temperature kilns. Lacking such an understanding, tradespeople in and
around Syria around the same time developed the craft of glassblowing, which allowed them to make
containers, jewelry, and windows. In both China and Syria, the skill of working with ceramics and glass
was developed by skilled craftspeople, ordinary citizens whose names have not been recorded. During
the era of the Roman Empire, glassblowing spread around the Middle East and Europe.

- Page 179 -

▶▶▶

Teaching Reasoning through Introductory Physics Lab Experiments

Worked glass was likely a fascination for those who saw it; however, only certain affluent men had the
means and opportunity to record their thoughts and ideas about it. In the second century CE, Claudius
Ptolemy (a Greek man with Roman citizenship who lived and worked in Egypt) suggested that when light
passed through glass the ratio of the incoming angle to the transmitted angle would always be the same.
In the year 984, Ibn Sahl (an Iranian man living in Iraq) conducted a series of experiments that led him to
propose that it is the ratio of the sines of these angles that is constant when light is refracted. Today, we
believe that Ibn Sahl's explanation does a better job of matching experimental results.
Ibn Sahl's explanation of refraction was independently rediscovered by Thomas Harriot (English) in 1602
and by Wildebrord Snellius (Dutch) in 1621. Snellius's work was publicized by Johannes Kepler
(German), which is why the idea is sometimes called Snell's Law. René Descartes (French, 1637) and
Pierre de Fermat (French, 1662) derived this relationship from mathematical principles, and Descartes
was the first to include the idea in a textbook that had wide circulation. Christiaan Huygens (Dutch, 1678)
showed how this ratio could be understood in terms of light waves.
I think there are two things to take away from this history. First, the development of a conceptual model
for refraction required a series of measurements and improvements, cycling through the modeling
framework several times as our predecessors developed increasingly refined understandings of the
phenomenon of refraction.
Second, the task of assigning credit for scientific inventions is challenging. Is it really appropriate to call
the explanation of refraction "Snell's Law"? What about Ibn Sahl, who discovered it more than 600 years
earlier? Furthermore, by focusing on published writing, are we ignoring the mountains of innovation done
by skilled craftspeople whose names are not remembered because they weren't rich or famous? What
about the role of the partners, families, and employees of famous scientists, upon whose support (and,
often, scientific expertise) they depended?
So, who benefits, and who loses out, when we talk about "Snell's Law"? Should it be called "Ibn Sahl's
Law" instead? And is it even reasonable to name scientific ideas after individuals?

predictions about our world. We can test those predictions by making comparisons or determining

our practices and knowledge in order to generate testable predictions.
Pseudoscience is a statement or practice that is presented as science, but is fundamentally
incompatible with the scientific practice of honestly testing predictions about the world.
Pseudoscience is common in our world today and includes unfalsifiable claims, undue secrecy
around experimental results, and public claims that are made without adequate justification.
Scientism is the view that science is the best, or only, way to understand the world, even in cases
where science might not be reasonably expected to apply. The term scientism can also be used
to describe disciplines of science that are used to try to explain aspects of the world for which that
discipline is clearly unsuitable.

Pseudoscience and scientism are significant issues in the world today. For example, pseudoscientific
vaccine hesitancy has led to outbreaks of measles, an unnecessarily steep death toll from COVID-19, and
increased rates of cervical cancer. Pseudoscientific climate change denialism is slowing our transition to a
green economy and risks exacerbating the effects of global warming. Likewise, scientism shows up in the
form of scientific racism, a long-discredited (but never quite vanquished) strand of thought that aims to
account for differences between races or ethnicities on the basis of biological or psychological difference,
rather than treating race as a social construct. Another example of scientism is when scientists try to
make statements about aesthetics, beauty, spirituality, or religion on the basis of scientific measurements.
We need to be careful to understand the limits of science. To that end, this lab focuses on an example of

How Electromagnetic Radiation Spreads Out in Air
T. M. Throckmorton
Electromagnetic radiation includes visible light, infrared, and other types of radiation.
By considering how electromagnetic radiation spreads out in air, I propose that
brightness and temperature both follow an inverse-square law with distance from a
light source.
The electromagnetic spectrum is the set of the different types of electromagnetic radiation generated
by changing electric and magnetic fields, including radio waves, infrared, visible light, and x-rays. A
glowing incandescent light bulb primarily emits visible light and infrared. Although we cannot see
infrared, we can infer its presence because it causes objects to warm up when it is absorbed. It is for
this reason that incandescent light bulbs, but not LED lights, get hot while they are switched on.

In order to explain how electromagnetic radiation spreads out through air, imagine that you quickly turn
a light bulb on, and then back off again. The pulse of light will travel out from the bulb through the air
like an expanding bubble. The bubble contains all the energy from the pulse of light, but as the bubble
expands the energy will be spread out across a larger area.
