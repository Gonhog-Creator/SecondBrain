Naval Nuclear Propulsion Program Overview
Mission
The mission of the Naval Nuclear Propulsion Program (NNPP), also known as Naval Reactors
(NR), is to provide militarily effective nuclear propulsion plants and ensure their safe, reliable,
and long-lived operation. This mission requires the combination of fully-trained men and women
with ships that excel in speed, endurance, stealth, and independence from logistics supply
chains.
NR Headquarters
NR is responsible for all aspects of the NNPP including research, design, construction, testing,
operation, and maintenance of the naval reactors aboard Navy ships and ultimate disposition of
spent naval reactor cores. This includes responsibility for Department of Energy facilities under
NNPP cognizance, regulatory authority for radiological controls involving naval nuclear
propulsion, and selection, training, and assignment of personnel. All of this work is
accomplished by dedicated research labs, nuclear-capable shipyards, equipment contractors
and suppliers, and training facilities, which are centrally controlled by a relatively small NR
Headquarters staff (~500 employees) in Washington, D.C.
NR operates under the authority granted by Executive Order 12344 as an integrated
organization within the Department of Energy (DOE) and the Department of the Navy (DON).
Approximately half of NR Headquarters employees are active duty military personnel, one fourth
are DON Federal civilians, and one fourth are DOE Federal civilians.
NR Headquarters Engineers (this could be you!) set standards and specifications for all
Program work, while our Field Office representatives monitor work at our prime contractors,
prototypes, and shipyards.
To carry out these responsibilities, NR Engineers function as technical managers in a wide
variety of disciplines including the following:

Engineer has responsibility for technical matters which can entail:

Based on over seven decades of engineering experience in nuclear propulsion, the NR
Headquarters organization exercises exacting control over all aspects of the Program,
demanding technical excellence and discipline unparalleled among nuclear programs.
Naval Reactors Field Offices
Naval Reactors has field offices located at its nuclear laboratories, its training facilities, and the
public and private shipyards certified to do NNPP work. Field Office representatives serve as
the “eyes and ears” of Naval Reactors Headquarters, performing oversight of contractors,
private industry, and Navy personnel to enforce compliance with government rules and NNPP
standards. NR has over 300 employees at Field Office sites.
Naval Nuclear Laboratory (NNL)
The NNL is a multi-site research, development, and support laboratory for all aspects of naval
nuclear propulsion. The NNL is owned by the government and operated by a contractor. Fluor
Marine Propulsion (FMP), a wholly owned subsidiary of Fluor, currently holds the contract to
staff and operate NNL sites. NR is FMP’s sole customer and controls the work performed by
NNL employees. FMP employs over 7,500 personnel at NNL sites.
Bettis Atomic Power Laboratory and Knolls Atomic Power Laboratory are the two largest NNL
sites by staff size. These NNL laboratory sites develop advanced naval nuclear propulsion
technology and provide technical support for the construction, operation, and maintenance of
naval nuclear propulsion plants.
NNL assists the Navy in training naval personnel on operational “prototype” reactors at the
Kesselring Site in West Milton, NY, and aboard Moored Training Ships in Charleston, SC.
NNL operates the Naval Reactors Facility (NRF) on the Idaho National Laboratory near Idaho
Falls, ID. NRF receives, examines, and prepares spent naval nuclear fuel for temporary storage.
Nuclear Component Procurement Organization
The NNPP has a dedicated prime contractor that is responsible for managing the Program’s
industrial base. Bechtel Plant Machinery, Inc. (BPMI), a privately owned Bechtel operating

division, currently holds this contract. NR is BPMI’s sole customer and controls the work
performed by their employees. BPMI has roughly over 1,000 employees.
BPMI is involved in the design, purchase, quality control, and delivery of major propulsion plant
components for installation in nuclear-powered submarines, aircraft carriers, and prototype
plants. The organization manages contracts with most of the key vendors used to fabricate
NNPP equipment. It has operations at two locations: Monroeville, PA, and Schenectady, NY.
NR Engineer Interview and Selection Process
The Navy commissions 15 NR Engineers each year through the NUPOC Program. NR conducts
a thorough screening and interview process to select this small cohort of high-potential
individuals. Before interviewing, interested applicants must coordinate with their recruiters to:
 Obtain medical clearance to commission in the Navy (known as an “N3M letter”)
 Initiate a security investigation
 Submit all application paperwork to Naval Reactors and receive a corresponding
Professional Recommendation (PROREC) to interview for the NR Engineer position

You will have separate technical interviews with three senior NR Engineers. Each
interview will last 45 to 60 minutes. Questions typically focus on technical topics you
studied in college or graduate school and, at a minimum, cover basic calculus and
calculus-based physics topics. In addition to technical topics, these interviewers will
probe your motivation to join the Navy, your understanding of the NR Program, and
other whole-person topics.
Based on your performance in initial technical interviews, you may be asked to conduct
a fourth technical interview.
You will have a final interview with the Director of Naval Reactors, a four-star Admiral.
This interview will last 5 to 20 minutes and will cover non-technical topics of the
Admiral’s choosing. He makes the ultimate hiring decision on the spot.

Before you conduct these formal interviews, you’ll have the opportunity to conduct an informal,
informational interview with an NR Engineer (typically from your alma mater or with the same
major). The purpose of the informational interview is to give you a chance to learn more about
NR (culture, expectations, job description, etc.) ahead of your formal interviews. Contact your
recruiter to schedule an informational interview at any point in your application process.

NR Engineer Commissioning and Training Pipeline
If the Admiral selects you to commission as an NR Engineer, you’ll sign official paperwork to
affiliate with the NUPOC Program and will begin to earn and accrue benefits ($15K hiring
bonus, monthly pay, healthcare, paid time off, etc.).
If you are still in school at this point, your NUPOC affiliation will boil down to these core
requirements: (1) go to school, (2) get good grades, (3) don’t do drugs, and (4) don’t end up in
jail. Your recruiter can cover specific NUPOC requirements with you separately. After
graduating, you will commission and begin the following training pipeline.
Officer Development School 5 Weeks
After commissioning you will attend Officer Development School (ODS) in Newport, Rhode
Island. This training is not specific to Naval Reactors. It provides all Restricted Line officers
(roughly equivalent to non-combat officers) with a basic introduction to Naval Leadership, Naval
Administration, Naval Organization, Sea Power, Military Law, Military Indoctrination, Naval
Warfare, and Damage Control.
Initial NR Engineer Technical Training (“S6G”) 9 weeks
After reporting to Naval Reactors, every Engineer will attend an initial nine-week compilation of
technical courses collectively referred to as “S6G.” S6G courses cover material ranging from
reactor physics to radiological controls and are designed to provide all Engineers a baseline
understanding of core Program technical topics before attending Bettis Reactor Engineering
School (BRES).
The training regimen includes 18 to 20 hours of classroom instruction per week with assigned
homework and exams. S6G is typically held twice a year, with classes beginning in March and
September. New Engineers are typically placed into the first class that convenes after they
report to NR.
Before S6G starts, Engineers will have a standard engineering workload commensurate with
their current level of training. Engineer workloads are tapered during S6G.
Prototype Training 2 weeks
After S6G and prior to BRES, Engineers will visit a prototype at the Kesselring Site in New York
or a Moored Training Ship in South Carolina for two weeks. Engineers will have the opportunity
to see and operate real nuclear equipment and systems. Training will consist of lessons,
demonstrations, and hands-on exercises coordinated by the plant staff with oversight from the
local field office.

Bettis Reactor Engineering School (BRES) 6 months
Every Engineer will attend BRES after completing S6G. BRES is taught twice a year at NR
Headquarters via VTC, with classes beginning in March and September. Engineers who begin
S6G in September will typically begin BRES the following March. Those who begin S6G in
March will likely attend BRES that September. Classes are taught by NNL employees and focus
on nuclear reactor design, construction, and operations.
Shipyard Training 3 weeks
Four public and two private shipyards service the Navy’s nuclear-powered submarines and
aircraft carriers. In general, our private shipyards build new submarines and aircraft carriers; our
public shipyards maintain them. NR conducts oversight of all of these processes. Immediately
following BRES, you will travel to one of our shipyards for three weeks to learn about NR’s
oversight functions from local Field Office personnel.
Expended Core Facility (ECF) Training 1 week
Immediately following Shipyard Training, Engineers will travel to the Naval Reactors Facility
(NRF) in Idaho to observe operations at the ECF. Following this training, Engineers will return to
Washington, D.C., where they will fulfill the remainder of their five-year service obligation.
Post-BRES Seminar Series (PBSS) 6 months
Every Engineer will complete the PBSS after returning to Washington, D.C. The series consists
of 28 lectures delivered between May and November every year. Each lecture lasts one to two
hours and is designed to help Engineers understand the roles and responsibilities of every
section at Naval Reactors.
Career Progression
Naval Reactors is a relatively flat organization with very few degrees of separation between the
Director and the most junior Engineer. The Headquarters is organized into roughly 20
departments (sections), each with its own department head who reports directly to Admiral
Caldwell. Every section is composed of smaller groups depending on the responsibilities of
each department, each managed by a Group Head. NR Engineers begin their careers reporting
directly to a Group Head.
Every Engineer is assigned to a headquarters section based on NR’s staffing needs and the
Engineer’s major. Engineers will likely remain in their initially assigned section for the duration of
their five-year military service obligation. Each section runs its own qualification process to
guide new Engineers towards meeting important career milestones.

Engineers promote along the nominal Junior Officer timeline, advancing to Lieutenant Junior
Grade (O-2) after two years of commissioned service (YCS) and to Lieutenant (O-3) after four
YCS. Associated pay scales are published online. NR Engineers are required to laterally
transfer to another officer community or resign at five YCS. There is no path for Engineers to
extend much beyond five YCS or to promote to LCDR. Limited lateral transfer options exist (see
below) so the majority of Engineers resign their commissions and either continue to work for NR
as civilians (in the Department of the Navy (DON) or the Department of Energy (DOE)) or seek
employment opportunities elsewhere. Many Engineers continue their Naval service in the
reserves.
Engineers who transfer into the DON or DOE are usually hired at the GS-14 level and earn a
starting salary of ~$125K a year before taxes. Once they have completed their initial five-year
military service obligation, Engineers are eligible to compete for various promotions and
transfers within Naval Reactors Headquarters and its Field Offices.
Lateral Transfer Options
(1) Naval Reactors Engineering Duty Officer
- The Naval Reactors Engineering Duty Officer Community is a special subset of the
wider Engineering Duty Officer Community. The Community does not receive
manning from direct accessions; it is completely manned by lateral transfers from the
1220 community.
- On average, one to two Engineers laterally transfer into the NR Engineering Duty
Officer Community every year. This is roughly 9% of the NR Engineer population
that reaches 5 YCS every year and must laterally transfer or resign.
- NR Engineering Duty Officers can promote up to CAPTAIN (O6) and complete a full
30-year career.
(2) Other. NR Engineers have recently had success laterally transferring into the following

Graduate Education Opportunities
Naval Reactors has a generous tuition assistance program, which employees can utilize to
pursue any training related to their work (including graduate-level courses).
BRES graduates receive the equivalent of a post-graduate certificate in Nuclear Engineering
from the NNPP. The following schools offer post-graduate transfer credits to BRES graduates:
Catholic University, Duke University, Naval Postgraduate School, North Carolina State
University, Old Dominion University, and Penn State University.
The NR Dress Code
NR Employees are rarely required to wear a uniform. New Engineers work in an office
environment where the dress code ranges from business formal to business casual attire.
