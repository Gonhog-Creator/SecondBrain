Wellness: New Health Goals
Healthy people seek a state of overall wellness rather
than merely the absence of illness.
• Health: the overall condition of body or mind and the
presence or absence of illness or injury.
• Wellness: optimal health and vitality, encompassing all
dimensions of well-being.
• Risk factor: a condition that increases one’s chances of
disease or injury.

The Dimensions of Wellness 2
Physical wellness includes your fitness level and your
ability to care for yourself.

Emotional wellness reflects your ability to understand
and deal with your feelings.
Intellectual wellness involves active, curious minds that
are able to detect problems and seek solutions to
questions about themselves and the larger world.
• People who enjoy intellectual wellness never stop
learning.

© McGraw Hill LLC

The Dimensions of Wellness 3
Interpersonal wellness includes the ability to develop
and maintain satisfying and supportive relationships.

Cultural wellness refers to the way you interact with
others who are different from you and includes
accepting, valuing, and celebrating different cultural
ways.
Spiritual wellness involves the guiding beliefs,
principles, or values that give meaning and purpose to
your life.
• It may be based in an organized religion or not.

© McGraw Hill LLC

Financial wellness is the ability to live within your
means and manage your money in a way that gives
you peace of mind.
Occupational wellness is the level of happiness and
fulfillment you gain through your work.

New Opportunities for Taking Charge 1
Life expectancy has roughly doubled since 1850.
In 1850, infectious disease and poor environmental
conditions shortened many lives.
• A person could expect to live only about 38–40 years.

The rate of improvement has slowed and, in some
recent years, actually declined—even without
factoring in the COVID-19 pandemic.

National Health
Wellness is a personal concern, but the U.S.
government has financial and humanitarian interests
in it too.
• A healthy population is the nation’s source of vitality,
creativity, and wealth.
• Poor health drains the nation’s resources and raises

Health Insurance Options 1
The Affordable Care Act (ACA) was signed into law in
2010.
• Although it remains in effect, certain provisions have
been altered—and insurance costs will likely increase as
a result.

Finding a health insurance plan:
• The ACA established health exchanges to facilitate
purchase of insurance; those below income requirements
are eligible for federal help with premiums.
• Often insurance plans are available from employers,
universities, and professional groups.
© McGraw Hill LLC

Health Insurance Options 2
Benefits to college students:
• Under the ACA, young adults can stay on their parents’
health insurance plans until age 26.
• Students who are young and healthy and who are
purchasing health insurance may be tempted by a
“catastrophic” plan—but this type of plan does not cover
many important types of care.
• Students whose income is below a certain level may

The Healthy People Initiative
Continuing a trend set by the national Healthy People
2020, Healthy People 2030 proposes these objectives:
• Attain healthy, thriving lives and well-being free of
preventable disease, disability, injury, and premature
death.
• Eliminate health disparities, achieve health equity, and
attain health literacy to improve the health and well-being
of all.
• Create social, physical, and economic environments that
promote attaining the full potential for health and wellbeing for all.
• Promote healthy development, healthy behaviors, and
well-being across all life stages.

Building Motivation to Change 1
Examine the pros and cons of change.
• Health behaviors have both short-term and long-term
benefits and costs.
• To be successful, you must believe that the benefits of
change outweigh the costs.
• Most people are more likely to be motivated by shorterterm goals.
• Over time, small differences can add up.

© McGraw Hill LLC

31

Building Motivation to Change 2
Boost self-efficacy.
• Self-efficacy: the belief in one’s ability to take action
and perform a specific task.
• Locus of control: the figurative “place” people
designate as the source of responsibility for events in
their life.
• Visualize yourself enjoying the benefits of change.
• Self-talk: a person’s internal dialogue.
• Role models and other supportive individuals can make
a big difference in your level of motivation and chances
of success.

© McGraw Hill LLC

Building Motivation to Change 3
Identify and overcome barriers to change.
• Don’t let past failures discourage you; they can be a
great source of information you can use.
• List the key barriers to change: the problems and
challenges you faced; and the short-term costs.
• Develop a practical plan for overcoming each barrier.

Enhancing Your Readiness to
Change
The transtheoretical, or “stages-of-change,” model is
an effective approach to lifestyle self-management.
•
•
•
•
•

Precontemplation: no intention of changing behavior.
Contemplation: intends to take action within six months.
Preparation: plans to take action within a month.
Action: outwardly changes behavior and environment.
Maintenance: successful behavior change is maintained
for six months or more.
• Termination: has exited the cycle of change and is no
longer tempted to lapse into old behavior.

Dealing with Relapse
People seldom progress through the stages of change
in a linear way.

Most people make several attempts before they
successfully change their behavior.
If relapse occurs:
• Forgive yourself.
• Give yourself credit for progress.
• Move on.

Being Fit and Well for Life
Your first attempts at making behavior changes may
never go beyond the contemplation or preparation
stage.
Once you have started, don’t stop.
Assume that health improvement is forever.
Take on the easier problems first, then use what you
learn to tackle more difficult problems later.

When you feel challenged, remind yourself that you
are creating a lifestyle that minimizes health risks and
maximizes your enjoyment of life.
