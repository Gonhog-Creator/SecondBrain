The purpose of this document is to summarize the efforts made by other teams in the space
specifically concerning thermals of RDE systems. The three main topics covered are cooling the
RDE, simulating and analyzing the system, and the implications of results.
To begin, the primary challenge with operating RDE’s at stable operating conditions for
any length of time is the high temperatures seen by the exhaust. Though highly dependent on
fuel selection, the majority of RDE’s produce exhaust gasses at a range of 1000 K to 3000 K,
with select models reaching 4000+ K. Due to manufacturing materials, most components
downstream of an RDE operating at 1500K+ would melt after several seconds of burn time.
There exists two options: cooling the air after it exits the combustion chamber or cooling the
combustion chamber directly.
Cooling the RDE can be done in many different ways. The most common and primitive
method is to not cool the system at all, simply limiting burn times to a second or less, leaving the
system time to cool to ambient temperature between runs. This of course limits the length of
runs and does not allow for observation at steady state, though has been done successfully for
a longer series of test runs by 1 2
Moving into active cooling, two primary methods exist: cooling the chamber itself and
cooling the exhaust. Water cooling the combustion chamber has promising literature, with many
teams extending runs and reaching a pseudo steady state. 3 4 Smallwood et. al. had runs
exceeding 50 seconds using the water cooling method, seeing inner body and outer body wall
temperatures of 300 and 250 degrees Fahrenheit, respectively.
Other than cooling the chamber itself, cooling the air using cold ambient air is also viable
to ensure survival of downstream components. Braun el al. utilized film cooling to both keep the
hot exhaust from contacting the chamber walls and introducing colder air, dropping
temperatures down from 2000K to 1500K. 5 A similar setup was used by Joest et. al in 2021,
utilizing this film cooling on both the inner and outer layers of the RDC. 6 Interestingly, adding
subsonic colder air could have more potential for our application as the flow would be both
colder and slower, leading to higher efficiencies in the downstream turbine.

combustion chamber, the plenum, and the turbine itself. Several teams have used simulations to

It should be said that the results of thermal analysis on the RDC and turbine will directly
result in design changes and operational guidelines. Using the heat flux from the exhaust and
the material for the turbine, we can estimate the maximum allowable time the turbine can be run
without causing irreversible blade damage or damage to other auxiliary systems, in addition to
keeping the RDC from melting. Being able to accurately simulate a thermal environment is
crucial to understanding and preventing key failure modes and will lead to a better designed
RDE.
