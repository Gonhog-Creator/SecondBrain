5. Course (Catalog) Description: Applications of cost accounting, cost estimation for new
equipment, manufacturing cost and measures of profitability. Use of computer simulation
design and cost programs. Procedures for sizing unit operations commonly encountered in
the chemical process industry. Heuristics for selection of separation processes and heat
exchanger network synthesis.
6. Prerequisite(s): Prerequisite: CHE 312; Corequisite: CHE 446 or CHE 447
7. Textbook(s) and/or Other Required Material: Turton, Shaeiwitz, Bhattacharyya and Whiting,
Analysis, Synthesis and Design of Chemical Processes, 5th Edition (Prentice Hall), ISBN
978-0134177403, Cost: $180
8. Course Learning Objectives:
1) Apply and synthesize information learned in previous courses toward the design and/or
operation of chemical processes
2) Apply concepts and calculations related to time value of money, cost components for plant
economics such as capital cost and manufacturing cost estimations, and/or typical
economic analysis methods such as net present value and return on investment
3) Use ASPEN to model typical chemical engineering unit operations

4) Develop team oriented performance criteria, problem-solving skills, and assessment
processes
5) Incorporate process safety considerations into process design
9. Class/Laboratory Schedule (# sessions/week and session duration): Two 75-minute lectures
per week.
10. Curricular Course Contribution: Engineering Topics (3 semester hours) with design content

13. Additional Course Information
•

Homework format is specified as follows (format is the similar to that used in CHE 205).
Points will be deducted from any homework submissions which do not follow this format:
a. Write on one side of each page
b. Write your name and the problem set number at the top of the first page of your
submission.
c. Begin each problem on a new page

d. Show all equations / calculations
e. Box all final answers
f. The problems should be submitted in the same order as in the homework assignment
•

Homework and some exam materials may be submitted in this class electronically by
submitting a scanned copy of your work via the Gradescope link provided on the course
Moodle site. Note the following policies; lack of adherence to any of these policies will
result in point deductions:
a. Submissions must be high-quality scans (not cell phone pictures) in order to
ensure legibility for grading. Be aware than typical “scanning” apps that come
with iPhones, etc. are often not suitable. It is suggested that students download
and use the free CamScanner app for Gradescope submissions; other options are
used by students at their own risk of point deductions.
b. When submitting scanned materials through Gradescope, submit your problems
and any subparts in the correct order. Be sure to select the correct pages in your
submission for each question. A tutorial showing how to adhere to these policies
is provided in the Gradescope Student Video Tutorial posted to the Moodle site.
c. For ASPEN problems, screen shots should be generated using an embedded
screen capture method (such as the "Snipping Tool" for PCs or Shift + Command
+ 4 for Macs). Screenshots which aren't clear, taken with a cell phone, etc. will
result in point deductions.
d. For ASPEN problems, provide the actual answer to the question being asked as
part of your submission (in other words, don't expect the grader to search for it on
your screenshot); points will be deducted if the answer to a question is not made
explicit.

•

Project deliverables will be submitted electronically in both of these two ways: (1)
delivered to your project mentor(s) via email or other means requested by your mentor,
and (2) by uploading one PDF (preferred format) or MS Word file of the deliverable to
the course’s TurnItIn.com website by the due time / date specified in the course syllabus
for the submission. Instances of plagiarism will be penalized on a situational basis and
may be referred to the Office of Student Conduct.

•

Any delay in report submission will decrease the report grade by 2% (and an additional
2% per each additional hour of delay, up to a maximum of 10% per calendar day).
Reports, etc. may be turned in electronically on weekends/holidays to avoid further
penalty.

•

Some capstone project mentors prefer that project deliverables are submitted in their
“final” state by the due date. Other capstone project mentors prefer that drafts of project
deliverables are submitted in advance so they can provide feedback for the team to
improve the final draft of the document submitted by the due date. In an effort to be
equitable in grading in light of these different mentor preferences, if a mentor asks for a
team to submit a draft of a deliverable in advance of the due date, on which they will
provide feedback for the team to incorporate into a final draft, both drafts of the report
will be graded, and the final team score on the deliverable will be an average of the

scores on the initial and revised reports. This policy applies to draft deliverables
submitted in whole or in part to the mentor for feedback before final submission. It
should be made clear that whether to grade and provide feedback on grade draft
deliverables provided in advance of the due date is entirely the choice of the mentor,
not the team.
•

Homework is due at the beginning of class; late submissions are penalized 10%. Each
additional 24 hours of delay after the corresponding deadline will decrease the grade by
an additional 10%. Homework submitted more than 48 hours late will not be accepted.

A free online response software called Top Hat (www.tophat.com) is a required

Apple or Android smartphones and tablets, laptops, or through text message. Any

used to engage the class, take attendance and/or count for participation. You can visit

in app support button, or by calling 1-888-663-5491.

•

Peer evaluation feedback must be submitted via CATME by the time the online survey
closes (typically 3-4 days after deliverables are submitted). CATME surveys will not be
redistributed for a group under any circumstances.

•

Dr. Cooper may ask a student group for a “long-form” peer evaluation to clarify CATME
scores if (1) one or more team members received a peer evaluation score more than 15%
below the group average and (2) little or no written explanation is given in CATME
student comments for the low peer evaluation score(s). Student teams can avoid longform evaluations by explaining peer evaluation scoring in comments through CATME.

•

Peer evaluation scores may be adjusted at the discretion of the instructor in circumstances
where it appears inappropriate team dynamics have prevented accurate peer evaluations.

•

The instructor may assign a grade of zero for a student’s deliverable if it is apparent the
student did not meaningfully contribute to the group assignment.

•

It is prohibited for students in different groups to share graded, ungraded or in-progress
course materials (including, but not limited to, reports, notes from Q&A sessions,
homework, etc.) in any format. Giving another student(s) any course materials without
instructor consent is considered "Aiding and Abetting" cheating as per NCSU's Code of
Student Conduct. In other words, if a student in another group is found to have
plagiarized your report, used your notes from Q&A sessions, etc., your grades will be
eligible to be reduced by the Office of Student Conduct (OSC). If this occurs after you
complete CHE 450, your grade is eligible to be retroactively reduced (possibly to an F,
which would void your CBE degree if you've already graduated).

Coordinating with other students, or individuals outside the course, on any assignments
and/or exams in any way can result in an academic integrity violation with the
university. Unauthorized aid additionally includes uploading or accessing on-line
solutions (e.g. Chegg), whether that involves posting problem solutions, accessing
posted solutions, or paying someone to assist in solving problems. All cases of
academic misconduct will be submitted to the Office of Student Conduct. Students
found guilty of academic misconduct will be subject to, at a minimum, a zero on the
assignment in question, up to a failing grade or a grade of incomplete (IN) for the
course, depending on the nature of the violation. In addition, if you are found guilty of
academic misconduct in the course, you will be on academic integrity probation for the
remainder of your years at NCSU and may be required to report your violation on
future professional school applications.

In the event a student misses one exam for any reason, they have the option to take a
comprehensive make-up exam in its place at the end of the semester, after the last week
of classes. Make-up exams submitted by students who missed an exam for a universityapproved reason are eligible for full credit. Make-up exams submitted by students who
missed an exam for a non-university-approved reason (e.g. flat tire, overslept, etc.) will
be penalized 30 points. If a student misses more than one exam, any additional exams
missed for a non-university-approved reason will receive a grade of zero; this policy
applies even if the first missed exam was for a university-approved reason.
