Reﬂections on Grading the Exams
HI 254-601, Fall 2024

The Essay (50 points on exam, but graded on 100 point scale)
I (Professor Mitchell) corrected all the essays. They were good! It was a challenging
question and most of you wrote essays that showed thought and a basic grasp of US
history since Reconstruction. Well done!
The most common problem was the lack of a clear thesis. Many of you diligently – and
often skillfully – recapped US history, but the prompt asked you to ANALYZE the past, not to
simply recount it.
The essays with no clear thesis could be impressive in their grasp of history but they
did not address the fundamental question: WHY did the expansion of US power abroad
and civil rights at home occur during the same time span?
Ideally you should have decided how to explain the rise of both trends – civil rights &
superpowerdom – and then written about where they overlapped and where they were in
opposition. Many of you focused on the rise of civil rights and barely explained how the
United States became a superpower.
There was no “right” answer. There are many, many possible theses.
For example, you could have written about how the following factors impacted both US
global power and civil rights (positively and negatively):
•

the development of the US economy from 1890-2010 led to both overseas
expansion and the rise of groups at home demanding their share of the wealth;

•

the wars (Spanish American, WW1, WW2, Korea, Vietnam, Terror) expanded both
US power and civil rights – but note, too, that civil liberties have been severely
limited in wartime (e.g., Espionage & Sedition Acts in WW1; internment of Japanese
in WW2; Patriot Act in War on Terror)
the US military – extending power abroad and in some ways at the forefront of
social change (integration in 1948; women in more prominent roles, including
combat in 2013.)

Americans asserting that they stood for freedom and equality this only became

One of you focused on the role that fear played both in the growth of US global

Both the road to superpowerdom and the expansion of civil rights have been very

You could also argue that there is a negative relationship between the rise to
superpowerdom & the expansion of civil rights, that US foreign escapades have
only hampered oppressed groups at home. Oppressed and marginalized people
have had to ﬁght tooth and nail to get any justice from the elites who always focus
on amassing power overseas.

I hope you can see that developing a thesis is not the same as simply summarizing
chronologically the period of US history we have studied. You needed to analyze WHY
both trends happened.

On a contemporary note, it was interesting to read your essays in a moment when the
Supreme Court has revoked the constitutional right to abortion and, eventually – possibly –
more. There is always a backlash to the expansion of civil rights, but for the Supreme Court
to take away a right is extraordinary. It is impossible not to worry that the era of the
expansion of civil rights that we have studied might be in the process of giving way to a
serious backlash and, perhaps, a new era. We’ll have to stay tuned… and vote!

The Cartoons (50 points)
Nay graded all the cartoons and wrote these comments.
Overall, I was impressed with your analyses of the cartoons! If you are confused about your
grade, the explanation could be:
•

You did not situate your analysis in any time period.

•

You misunderstood what the cartoon was depicting and/or the point the cartoonist
was making. I got the impression that those of you who may not have paid
attention to the lectures struggled with some of the cartoons, all of which were
used in Professor Mitchell’s lectures.

Common errors for speciﬁc cartoons were:
Cartoon 1 “Rwanda”
● No mention of the US (Clinton administration) response to the Rwandan genocide.
Some of you never mentioned Rwanda.
● Not understanding that the world, including the Clinton administration, was fully
informed that the genocide was occurring.
Cartoon 2 “Next!”
● Failure to place the cartoon in the late 19th/early 20th century, when Roosevelt was
ﬁghting trusts. (The only thing not in the creature’s grasp is the White House!)
Cartoon 3 “New Member of the Orchestra”
● Missing the context of the Spanish-American War - this is what ushers the United
States into the “orchestra” along with the European imperialist powers
● If you missed this context you got a “0” on the cartoon.
Cartoon 4 “Reagan and Gorbachev”
● A few people misunderstood the cartoonist’s irony - the cartoon is not praising
Reagan; it is saying that Gorbachev is the “real hero” behind the end of the Cold
War, but Reagan gets all of the credit. Also, some of you asserted, wrongly, that
Gorbachev sought the union of East & West Germany; he did not seek it.
● Some people did not explain the historical context clearly.

Cartoon 5 “What a Lucky Thing We’ve Got Separate Beds!”
● Failure to explain the context of the isolationist movement in the late 1930s/early
1940s.
● The irony of the closeness of the two beds - Seuss is making fun of the
isolationists; he is showing that the “contagions” in Europe are right next to us.

Final thoughts
Nay and I have been amazed and impressed by the discipline and seriousness that you
have applied to this course. We know it takes extra effort to keep up with an asynchronous
zoom class. So we salute you and hope you have a restful, healthy and happy holiday. To
those of you who are graduating, hearty congratulations! Please email me if you have any
questions about the essays and cartoons.
When I called this “Final” thoughts, I meant it: I am retiring and this is my last class after 30
years at NC State. You have been a great class to end on. Thank you!
