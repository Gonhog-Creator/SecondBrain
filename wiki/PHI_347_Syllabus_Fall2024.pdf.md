Office Hours: W 1:30-2:30 PM and by appointment, Withers Hall 453
Catalog Description: Introduction to principal theoretical, empirical and normative issues
at the intersection of neuroscience and philosophy, including such issues as: consciousness,
the mind’s “I” and the brain’s “I”; free will, moral responsibility and neuroscience; the ethics
of personal enhancement; brains, human nature and personal identity; neuroscientifically
informed evaluation of well-being.
--Neuroscience is making significant discoveries and developments at a rapid pace and
increasing our knowledge of the mind/brain. This course is intended to introduce students
to the fields of inquiry at the intersection between neuroscience and philosophy, including
its normative, descriptive and theoretical foci and methods. The course is designed so that
students will gain both knowledge and skills in practical and theoretical issues. Students will
navigate general review texts, as well as empirical and normative texts during the course.
Some of these will be oriented towards describing the science, while others lean towards
conceptual analysis and normative claims arising from the use of neuroscientific knowledge.
Furthermore, the learning experiences should be enhanced through discussions, critical
evaluation of conceptual and empirical papers, and an essay exam at the end.
Pre-requisites, co-requisites, enrollment restrictions: none
GEP: satisfies 3 cr hrs of either Humanities or Interdisciplinary Perspectives requirements
GEP Humanities Objectives and Outcomes
This course will provide instruction and guidance that help students to:
1. Engage the human experience through the interpretation of human culture and
2. Become aware of the act of interpretation itself as a critical form of knowing in the
humanities; and
3. Make academic arguments about the human experience using reasons and evidence
for supporting those reasons that are appropriate to the humanities.
Outcomes:
For Objective 1: Students will be able to describe a variety of philosophical conceptions of
the mind, self and consciousness addressed by contemporary cognitive neuroscience and
to assess the plausibility of different approaches taken by the sciences in attempting to
explain those philosophical conceptions.
Measures for 1: Essays on lecture topics, Midterm and Final exam questions.
(Sample question: How has mind/body dualism informed judgments of moral
responsibility? What would be the effect on those judgments of rejecting dualism in
favor of an alternative view grounded in cognitive neuroscience?)

For Objective 2: Students will be able to apply philosophical methods to evaluate the
application of neuroscientific explanations to ethical and social implications (e.g. Are
arguments intended to uncover truth or overcome opponents?).
Measures for 2: Essays on lecture topics, Midterm and Final exam questions.
(Sample question: How do some of what’s been learned about the human central
nervous system promise or threaten to challenge traditional valuing of altruistic
behavior and motivating compassion?)
For Objective 3: Analyze and reconstruct neuroscientific arguments concerning the
supposed basis of the mind in the brain and the alleged further implications for ethics.
Measures for 3: Essays on lecture topics, Midterm and Final exam questions.
(Sample question: Are there some kinds of neurological disorders that would under
some circumstances absolve a person suffering from them of moral responsibility
for particular kinds of actions? What would be a good example of neurological
disorders, circumstances and actions in question?)
GEP Interdisciplinary Perspectives Objectives and Outcomes:
The course will also provide instruction and guidance in interdisciplinary perspectives that
help students to:
1. Distinguish between the distinct approaches of philosophy and neuroscience; and
2. Identify and apply authentic connections between philosophy and neuroscience;
and
3. Explore and synthesize the approaches or views of philosophy and neuroscience.
For Objective 1: Students will be able to identify and differentiate the contributions of
neuroscience and philosophy to the development of important questions concerning
meaning and responsibility.
Measures for 1: Performance on in-class presentation, essays on lecture topics, midterm exam and the final exam. (Sample question: What are the ethical implications
of claims from neuroscience about the neural basis of conscious volition or free
will?)
For Objective 2: Students will be able to describe and analyze the strengths and
weaknesses of the neurophilosophical model of the mind/brain both for philosophical rigor
and empirical grounding in neuroscience.
Measures for 2: Performance on in-class presentation, essays on lecture topics, midterm exam and the final exam. (Sample question: How does the integrative
neurophilosophical model of the mind/brain explain or explain away traditional
intuitions about the nature of consciousness and subjective experience?)
For Objective 3: Students will be able to distinguish, analyze and critically evaluate
theoretical, normative and empirical claims in neuroscience and philosophy.
Measures for 3: Performance on the mid-term exam, final exam topical focus
proposal and the final exam itself. [Sample question: Identify three methodologically
and ethically debatable uses of neuroimaging and say how they relate to the
historical development of brain science (e.g., phrenology).]

Course Student Learning Outcomes:
By the end of the course, students will be able to:
1. Identify major empirical, normative (ethical and non-ethical) and methodological
issues in the intersection of neuroscience and philosophy;
2. Determine which approaches are effective in analyzing these issues;
3. Differentiate among levels of explanation and modeling in neuroscience as the levels
affect potential practical applications of any neuroscientific hypotheses;
4. Distinguish empirical from theoretical justifications (both of which are typically
needed) in evaluating proposed resolutions of these issues.

Attendance/Absence Policy: Attendance is required and will be recorded each class
period. You may miss two classes without a documented excuse. After that threshold, your
final grade will be penalized. Specifically, unexcused absences are calculated as follows: 3
absences (final grade lowered by a third, e.g. from an A to an A-); 4 absences (final grade
lowered by two thirds, e.g. from an A to an B+); 5 absences (final grade lowered by a whole
letter grade, e.g. from an A to B); 6 or more absences (automatic F for the course, regardless
of work completed). Excused absences, following the university definition of an excused
absence, must be documented properly. Please review the university attendance regulation
at http://policies.ncsu.edu/regulation/reg-02-20-03 for further information, including the
university definition of excused absences. Please contact the instructor immediately if you
have a conflict to arrange a deadline for a makeup assignment (e.g., a 750-word summary
of required readings for each missed class, regardless of reason).
Late Assignments. Late work will be marked down one full letter grade per day.
