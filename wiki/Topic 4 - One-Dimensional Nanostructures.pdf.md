Growth on a Flat Surface (KSV theory)
Adatom (typically with one bond) diffuses randomly until it reaches an
energetically favorable site ➔ irreversible incorporation leading to
growth
Different sites have different energy:
– On terrace ➔ one bond (adatom) (7)
– At step edge (ledge site) ➔ two bonds (8)
– At ledge-kink site ➔ three bonds (5)
– At kink site ➔ four bonds (4)
– In terrace ➔ five bonds (1 & 6)
– When step edge buries terrace ➔ six bonds

Growth proceeds by advancement of steps, AKA step flow

Developed by Hartman and Perdok

– Flat surfaces
– Stepped surfaces
– Kinked surfaces

Difference between facets: number of

Topic 4 - 16

Anisotropic Growth
Generally:
– S and K have adsorption-limited growth, since accommodation is
complete
– Accommodation in F ranges from 0 to 1, depending on availability of
kink and ledge sites

Note: Facets with fast growth rate (high surface energy) tend to
disappear, just as in a crystal in thermodynamic equilibrium (Wulff plot)
Consequence: formation of nanostructures with high aspect ratio is
limited to materials with special crystal structures
Anisotropic growth is achieved at low supersaturation, such that P or C
is higher than P0 or C0 (saturation pressure and concentration,
respectively) for the growth surface and lower than P0 or C0 for nongrowth surfaces
Also, anisotropic growth may require other mechanisms, such as
defect-induced growth and/or impurity-inhibited growth

Dissolution-Condensation
Differs from E-C only in growth media
Growth species first dissolve into solvent or solution, diffuse through
liquid and deposit onto the surface
Growth via homogeneous or heterogeneous nucleation
Examples:
– Spherical colloids of amorphous Se (300 nm) prepared through
reduction of selenious acid with excess hydrazine at 100oC; solution
cooled to RT ➔ Se nanocrystals (trigonal) precipitated; solution is aged

Growth
A secondary material (impurity or catalyst) used to direct/confine crystal

Role of catalyst:
– liquid drop traps and/or alloys with growth species
– Supersaturation of growth species ➔ precipitation at growth surface,

First demonstration (45 yrs ago):
– Wagner fabricated Si nanowires/whiskers by VLS

Wagner’s observations:

– In Si, <111> is the slowest direction as compared with low index

– Impurities are always required
– A liquid-like globule is found at the tip of the nanowire

Principles of VLS Growth
Catalyst or impurity must form a liquid solution with
growth species at deposition temperature
Distribution coefficient of catalyst < 1 at deposition
temperature
Low equilibrium vapor pressure of catalyst is
preferred, as loss of material could lead to reducing
diameter of nanowire; catalyst can be supplied during
growth
Catalyst must be chemically inert with regards to
growth species and by-products
Interfacial energy is critical; wetting characteristics
influence diameter. For a given volume, small wetting
angle ➔ large growth area and large diameter.
Smaller volume ➔ smaller diameter
For compound nanowire growth, one of the
constituents can serve as catalyst
Unidirectional growth requires that solid-liquid
interface have a well-defined crystallography (use
single crystal substrates)

Electrochemical Deposition
Deposition of materials (conductive materials) via electrochemical process,
AKA electrodeposition or electroplating:
– Oriented diffusion of charged growth species (cations) through an electric field
– Reduction of charged growth species at the deposition surface (electrode)

Limited to deposition of conductors: metals, alloys, semiconductors and
conducting polymers, because deposit must act as electrode after initial
deposition
When deposition confined to pores of template membranes ➔
nanocomposites; if template membrane is removed ➔ nanorods and
nanowires

Ni deposition into polycarbonate
membrane with 60 nm pores at -1V.

Electrophoretic Deposition
Deposition on cathodes from colloidal dispersion
Used for ceramics and organoceramic materials (no need for conducting material)
Nanoparticles/nanorods in colloidal dispersion are stabilized (electrostatic/electrosteric)
When placed in a polar solvent or electrolyte solution, electrical charge develops on NP
surface
Charged surfaces electrostatically attract oppositely charged species (counter-ions)
When an electric field is applied, the charged particles are set in motion ➔
electrophoresis
Electrophoretic deposition ➔ uses oriented motion of charged particles to grow films on
the electrode. If the particles are positive ➔ deposition at cathode

Self-Shadowing Methods
Material deposition from a source in
high and ultrahigh vacuum conditions
tends to be a “line of sight” process,
where atoms travel in a straight line
from the source to the substrate
At oblique incidence conditions, a
geometric shadow is cast behind
topographic features, like islands on a
flat surface. This results in a preferential
capturing of atoms by topographically
prominent features

DOI: 10.1155/2012/689154

If surface diffusion is low, the
shadowing length dominates the length
scale of the features as they grow
Geometric motion of the substrate
relative to the source allows for
sophisticated control over the geometry
of nanoscale features

DOI: 10.1007/978-3-642-40128-2
