Project Purpose

I.A Purpose
Developing clean energy technology is a major focus of current research. New methods in
combustion, particularly pressure gain combustion, offer potential solutions. There exists several types of
engines that fulfill this requirement. Of note, rotating detonation combustors (RDCs), which use a
detonation (supersonic flame front) rather than a deflagration to complete combustion, represent an
opportunity to achieve greater efficiency goals in power generation. However, these systems face
significant challenges due to the fluctuating exhaust produced by RDCs, which induce low supersonic
flows and large flow angle fluctuations. These unsteady inflows severely impact the performance of
downstream conventional turbines, causing increased pressure losses, unstable operation, shock wave
formation, and reduced power output. Additionally, high heats generated during detonation make
installing infrastructure downstream for power generation a design challenge. There is a need for turbine
designs optimized to handle these challenges, as well as the high heat flux levels found within RDCs.
Without effective cooling strategies, such as stepped axial injection cooling slots and nitrogen gas film
cooling, the high heat flux levels pose a significant risk to the efficiency and lifespan of turbines. The
purpose of this project is to advance turbine systems by optimizing their performance with RDC flows,
maximizing power generation, and addressing critical challenges like shock interaction with turbine
blades and thermal management.
Funded and sponsored by James Braun, assistant professor at North Carolina State University
researching supersonic propulsion methods, this project aims to provide a state-of-the-art advancement in
RDC power generation. By leveraging his research and resources, our team will work to optimize turbine
systems for RDC applications, ultimately contributing to the broader use of RDCs in various industries.
The benefits of this work include improving the thermodynamic efficiency of power generation systems
such as power plants and aircraft engines. This will lead to reduced environmental impact and help meet
the growing global power demand. Additionally, the advancements made in this project will pave the way
for future research and industrial applications involving RDCs, providing society with new resources to
further enhance energy efficiency.

I.B.1 Turbine
​
The history of turbines dates back centuries to the earliest of turbines: water wheels. They were
able to harvest the energy from a moving stream to assist with tasks on the land, such as grinding grain.
By 200 BCE, vertical-axis windmills, another form of turbine, were in use in Persia to pump water.
Turbines eventually spread throughout Europe, where they were used for similar purposes. During the
Industrial Revolution, turbine development accelerated, mainly with the use of steam power. Charles

Parsons was credited for the invention of the steam turbine in 1884. Parson’s design improved efficiency
in electricity generation and marine propulsion, pushing a more efficient power generation into the future.
​
In terms of aircraft propulsion, the gas turbine represented a breakthrough in propulsion
technology. Early jet propulsion can be traced back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with
engineers wanting to achieve high-speed and efficient flight. The jet engine came from Sir Frank Whittle
in the UK, who patented a jet engine design in 1930. His invention had a centrifugal compressor and an
axial turbine, capable of producing continuous thrust by expelling exhaust gases out the nozzle. In
Germany, Hans von Ohain developed a similar turbojet, leading to the first jet-powered flight of the
Heinkel He 178 in 1939​. This progress was only accelerated during World War II, where jet powered
aircraft became the dominant fighter in the airspace. Today, large turbofans are used to provide thrust for
jet airlines and military aircraft. Modern advancements have drastically increased the propulsion power
and efficiency of turbines and it is only going to continue to accelerate in the later 21st century [1].
​
Current turbine designs for subsonic flow applications are highly established. In subsonic
turbofan engines, turbine stages are optimized for converting high-energy exhaust from the combustor
into mechanical work that drives the fan and compressor. High-pressure turbine stages typically handle
the highest temperatures and pressures, requiring advanced materials such as single-crystal superalloys
and thermal barrier coatings to withstand extreme conditions. Low-pressure turbines consist of multiple
stages with larger, aerodynamically efficient blades to extract energy efficiently from lower-energy
exhaust, ultimately powering the bypass fan in turbofan engines. The design focus is on minimizing
weight and maximizing efficiency, often using 3D aerodynamics to reduce losses.
​
In industrial gas turbines for power generation, turbine stages are designed for sustained
high-efficiency operation over a range of loads. Stages are engineered with robust cooling systems, such
as film and convection cooling, to manage thermal stresses. Blade profiles are aerodynamically optimized
to minimize turbulence and maximize energy extraction from the expanding gases. Advances in materials
science, including ceramic matrix composites, enable turbine blades to operate at higher temperatures,
improving efficiency while maintaining durability​.
Modern innovations in turbine stages focus on improving performance and sustainability.
Aerodynamic improvements, such as non-axisymmetric end walls, reduce secondary flow losses in both
stators and rotors, boosting efficiency. Computational fluid dynamics tools are used to design blades with
complex shapes, such as swept or bowed profiles, which improve load distribution and flow management.
Active flow control systems, using techniques like blowing or suction, are being explored to further
enhance efficiency and extend operational ranges. Additive manufacturing is also useful for creating
cooling channel designs within turbine blades, providing superior heat management compared to
conventional methods [2]

In aircraft lubrication systems, the process begins with a dry sump reservoir located at the bottom

before returning it to the sump. Meanwhile, the oil cooler reduces the oil temperature by passing it
through a matrix that exchanges heat with either fuel (preheating the fuel) or air, completing the cycle

I.B.3 Shafts and Bearings
​
The shaft and bearing system is a critical component in many power generation applications. The
shaft serves as the primary conduit for transferring rotational energy, while the bearings support the shaft
and minimize frictional losses. Bearings predate shafts, as primitive humans used rolling motions, such as
logs, to reduce friction when moving heavy objects. Another early example comes from ancient Egyptian
civilizations, where sleds with water-lubricated runners were used to transport heavy slabs, effectively
functioning as bearings [6].
The early use of shafts in power generation dates back to around 100 BC with the invention of the
"Aeolipile" by Hero of Alexandria, as shown in Fig. 6. This device utilized a heat source to boil water
within a sphere, increasing pressure inside the chamber and causing rotational motion, theoretically
generating power. In this setup, the wetted interface between the device and the shaft also acted as a
bearing surface, reducing friction [7]

Figure 4. Aeolipile Designed by the Hero of Alexandria[6]
​
Advancements in these mechanisms progressed during the Renaissance, with Leonardo da Vinci
designing devices such as the "Chimney Jack," which used airflow in a chimney to rotate a turbine for
turning a spit [7]. Leonardo also conceptualized ball bearings in his helicopter design [6]. During the
Industrial Revolution, Philip Vaughan patented the first official ball bearing in 1792 [6], and engineers
like Carl Gustav De Laval, Sir Charles Parsons, and Auguste Rateau made significant advancements in
steam turbines. Innovations in shaft and bearing designs allowed for variable diameters to regulate load
and constraints and for different bearing types to secure shafts in various ways. Despite these
advancements, early steam turbines often struggled with inefficiencies due to the energy required to heat
water for steam production.
By the early 20th century, engineers like Charles G. Curtis addressed these inefficiencies with the

Rolling bearings are subdivided into roller and ball bearings. Roller bearings, which use
cylindrical rollers instead of balls, are designed to distribute and support larger loads. These bearings can
be further classified into three types: thrust, angled/tapered, and radial bearings. Thrust bearings provide
axial support for the shaft, with their inner and outer races oriented axially to handle axial loads
effectively. Angled or tapered bearings, featuring a flat inner race and an angled outer race, provide
balanced support for both axial and radial loads. Radial bearings, on the other hand, focus primarily on
supporting loads perpendicular to the shaft’s axis. Rolling bearings are commonly used in
turbomachinery, with at least one bearing type typically employed to support axial loads depending on the
specific application requirements.
Journal bearings, also known as fluid film bearings, operate without rotating components on the
bushing or bearing. Instead, the journal (shaft) rotates within a clearance of 1.5–2 thousandths of an inch
per inch of journal diameter. This clearance is filled with a pressurized oil film, which not only allows the
shaft to rotate freely but also supports the shaft loading [3]. The bushing is housed within a support
structure that secures the bearing and provides channels for oil supply.
Both journal and rolling bearings continue to evolve, with their configurations and materials
tailored to meet the demanding requirements of modern turbomachinery, including high rotational speeds,
heavy load distribution, and precise operational performance.
I.B.4 Starter Generator
​
The need for starting systems dates back to the early 20th century, coinciding with the
development of gas turbines. Early gas turbines, such as the Holzwarth explosion turbine developed in the
1900s, utilized external means to initiate rotation, often relying on compressed air or auxiliary engines.
These methods were cumbersome and lacked efficiency. In the mid-20th century, the integration of
electric starter motors became more prevalent. For instance, the Neuchâtel gas turbine, operational in
1939, employed electric starters to initiate turbine rotation. However, these systems were separate from
the generators, leading to increased weight and complexity. The development of combined
starter-generator systems marked a significant advancement. These systems functioned as starters during
engine initiation and transitioned to generators during operation, optimizing space and weight. This dual
functionality became particularly advantageous in aerospace applications, where efficiency and
compactness are paramount.
Modern starter-generator systems represent a significant evolution from the older, separate starter
and generator configurations [9]. Unlike traditional systems that required standalone starters and
generators, modern integrated starter-generators (ISGs) combine both functions into a single unit,
reducing system weight and complexity. For instance, advancements in control strategies, such as those
outlined in U.S. Patent No. 7253535B2, allows for seamless transitions between starter and generator
modes, optimizing torque profiles and minimizing transients [10]. Modeling of these systems, such as in
Simulink or other computational platforms, has further enabled engineers to predict performance, refine
control algorithms, and design for reliability under various operating conditions [11]. These
developments, coupled with the adoption of high-speed permanent magnet and switched reluctance
machines, demonstrate the ongoing push towards more compact, efficient, and high-performance systems
in gas turbine applications. The integration of such advanced control and modeling techniques has

II.D Functional Block Diagram
The functional block diagram illustrates the key components and systems involved in the
operation of a rotating detonation turbine generator. At its core, the system includes the RDC (Rotating
Detonation Combustor), which is responsible for the detonation process that powers the turbine. The
RDC's instrumentation collects necessary data, such as high and low frequency pressure measurements
and temperature readings via thermocouples, feeding this information to the data acquisition system. The
detonation chamber produces exhaust gasses that drive the turbine blades. The turbine converts the kinetic
energy from the exhaust into mechanical energy, rotating the shaft, which is connected to a generator. The
mechanical systems, including the oil feed and shaft, are designed to create smooth and safe power
generation. Torque and RPM sensors will track the turbine and generator's rotational speed and power
output. Additional sensors, such as a voltmeter and ammeter, will monitor electrical performance,

fluctuations in the exhaust caused by detonation waves, allowing the chance for detailed analysis of the
detonation process and its impact on blade stability and efficiency. By recording these fast-paced events,
the cameras provide critical data on the behavior of the detonation waves and the exhaust flow dynamics
as they interact with the turbine. In addition, regular cameras will be used for broader visual monitoring,
continuously verifying the overall system performance and ensuring all components are functioning as
expected. Together, these cameras are crucial for both analysis and real-time operational safety.

cameras, allowing for real-time analysis. The Command Center oversees system control, data analysis,

III.B Turbine
III.B.1 Key Design Alternatives
The purpose of the turbine is to extract energy from the hot exhaust flow emanated from the
RDC. This energy will be used to turn a generator in order to produce power. For conventional turbines,
the blades are subjected to the subsonic combustion products from the combustor. However, the
supersonic exhaust from RDCs imposes many challenges, mainly due to shock formations on the blades
and high material stresses during hotfires. Selection of the type of turbine was based on several factors
and two main designs were considered.
A turbine stage has two sections: A stator and a rotor. The purpose of the stator is to turn the flow
at the optimal angle that the rotor needs, producing the maximum amount of power possible. Stator vanes
do not move and are historically used before any rotor section. Rotors are the device that rotates and
extracts work for the high energy flow. The main focus of the trade study is to determine the rotor type,
which in turn correlates to the style of stator that will be used.
For the purpose of power generation, radial turbines work by collecting the flow axially and
expanding in a tangential direction. This setup is called axial-inflow/radial outflow. Generally, radial
turbines are suited for more compact turbine setups, as they are capable of higher pressures drops across a
single turbine assembly when compared to an axial stage.

conjugate heat transfer simulations. This will enable an accurate topographical map of the heat seen by
downstream components.
Because of these extreme temperatures and pressures, any components downstream that will be in
contact with the exhaust will need to be robust and must be able to withstand the extreme temperature
ranges for the lifetime of testing. While active cooling has been proven to be the most successful for
longer duration testing, allowing the thermal capacity of downstream components to heat up and cool
down between testing (usually referred to as a heat sink method) has also been shown to be viable. An
analysis on the most common cooling methods and their reasonings are shown below.
The first type of thermal control used in RDE systems is a heat sink. Simply put, the system is not
cooled actively by any means, instead, the system is limited to short run times, limiting the time
components are in contact with hot exhaust. This has several advantages and disadvantages. Firstly, the
testing run times are severely limited, usually lasting between 0.2 and 1.5 seconds in order to keep the
system from experiencing permanent thermal deformation or failure. This severely limits the ability to test
the system at thermal steady state, which is the regime most applications will use. Because of this, a heat
sink method is commonly used to show a “proof of concept,” instead of as a feasible design. Despite this
disadvantage, the cost is practically zero, as neither additional equipment is required nor must the system
be designed in such a way to incorporate a cooling system. In addition to the details above, the testing
architecture must be designed around the system cooling down between runs. For testing with timescales
of several days between tests, this is not a big concern, though for back to back runs, thermal management
must be considered. One solution to this issue is to force cold or room temperature air through the system
between tests, allowing for the ambient temperature to cool the components. This is also useful to
accelerate components downstream of the combustion chamber if the testing process requires [11].

The second type of thermal control used in RDE systems is film cooling. This method seeks to

reduction, no cooling is assumed and therefore integration of a turbine calls for a starter. To maximize the

V.A.2 Literature Review
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has played a crucial role in advancing the design and
performance of gas turbines. By providing detailed insights into aerodynamics, heat transfer, and
combustion processes, CFD enables engineers to optimize turbine efficiency, minimize losses, and
improve cooling mechanisms. Research has focused on various aspects of gas turbine performance,
including turbulence modeling, cooling strategies, combustion analysis, and overall efficiency
enhancement.
Turbulence modeling is essential for accurately predicting aerodynamic losses and secondary
flow effects in gas turbines. Denton provided a foundational analysis of loss mechanisms in
turbomachinery, emphasizing the role of secondary flows and tip leakage in reducing efficiency [1]. More
recent studies have explored the application of Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes , Large Eddy
simulation, and hybrid RANS-LES models for improved accuracy in capturing flow separation and vortex
structures [1]. Dawes introduced three-dimensional computational methods that enhanced turbulence
predictions, laying the groundwork for high-fidelity CFD simulations [2].​

Cooling techniques are critical for extending the lifespan of gas turbine blades, which operate
under extreme thermal conditions. Rigby et al. conducted CFD analyses of film cooling effectiveness,
demonstrating how different cooling hole configurations influence thermal efficiency [4]. The integration
of conjugate heat transfer (CHT) models has further improved predictions of thermal stresses and material
degradation in high-pressure turbine blades. Han et al. reviewed various cooling strategies, including
impingement cooling, effusion cooling, and transpiration cooling, highlighting advancements in CFD
modeling that have enhanced cooling performance [3]. Cooling was not a large part of our project work.

Combustion modeling in gas turbines is a complex area where CFD is extensively used to predict
flame behavior, pollutant formation, and combustion instabilities. Unsteady simulations using LES and
Direct Numerical Simulation have provided deeper insights into flame dynamics and acoustic
interactions. Pitsch explored detailed chemical kinetics modeling, improving the accuracy of NOx and
CO emission predictions [5]. Furthermore, CFD has been applied to study alternative fuels such as
hydrogen and biofuels, assessing their impact on turbine efficiency and emissions. For this project,
combustion modeling was not as important compared to other methods.

CFD is also instrumental in optimizing gas turbine performance by refining blade profiles and
minimizing aerodynamic losses. Schobeiri provided an extensive review of turbomachinery flow physics,
discussing how CFD has been used to enhance turbine efficiency [6]. Machine learning techniques
integrated with CFD have recently emerged as a promising approach for optimizing turbine geometries

The design and validation of the starter-generator subsystem for the Rotating Detonation Turbine
Generator (RDTG) system rely on a comprehensive understanding of motor modeling, experimental
testing, and integration challenges. This section provides an overview of key research in these areas,
focusing on brushless DC motor models, validation methods, and the integration of the starter-generator
with the system's rotating mechanical components.
Brushless DC motors require accurate modeling to predict performance under both steady-state
and transient conditions. Traditional three-constant models, which consider resistance, back-emf constant,
and no-load current, are commonly used but often fail to capture losses due to eddy currents in motors
with thick laminations. To address this limitation, Carri introduced a four-constant model that
incorporates additional parameters for eddy current losses, enabling more accurate predictions of motor
behavior. Gabriel et al. extended this approach by experimentally validating the four-constant model for
UAV propulsion systems, demonstrating its utility in practical applications. Mellor et al. proposed a
computationally efficient loss model for brushless AC machines, leveraging magnetostatic finite element
analyses to predict iron losses under rated and field-weakening conditions.
Experimental validation remains a cornerstone of motor modeling. Gong et al. conducted detailed
performance testing of BLDC motors and speed controllers, emphasizing the importance of integrating
experimental data with simulation models to improve accuracy. Similarly, Lundström et al. highlighted
the role of small-scale electric propulsion system validation in ensuring reliable operation under diverse
conditions. These studies underscore the need for rigorous testing to bridge the gap between theoretical
models and real-world performance.
The integration of the starter-generator subsystem with the RDTG system introduces additional
challenges due to the unsteady nature of RDC exhaust. Ji et al. investigated the performance impacts of
RDCs on turbofan systems, noting the unique pressure and temperature fluctuations that must be managed
in downstream components. This insight is crucial for designing motor controllers and regenerative
systems capable of handling dynamic loads during operation.

V.D.2 Literature Review
Cold flow testing is a critical methodology for evaluating the performance of turbines and propulsion
systems, offering a controlled, non-reactive environment to assess aerodynamic and mechanical behavior
before advancing to high-pressure and high-temperature conditions. This approach is especially critical
for systems integrated with rotating detonation engines (RDEs), which impose unique challenges due to
their high thermal efficiency and unsteady exhaust flows. The cold flow tests will be conducted using a
modular nozzle system belonging to BE-FAST Labs. Cold flow is an intuitive method of testing with use
cases in a variety of aerospace focuses. An example being the cold flow test of liquid rocket engines. One
reason for evaluating rocket engines using a cold flow is to evaluate injector performance and sealing . By
simulating non-reacting conditions, the study demonstrated how cold flow tests enable precise evaluation
of injector dynamics, such as atomization and mixing. Diakunchak (1989) explored the use of cold flow
rig tests for redesigning industrial compressor turbines. The study highlighted the importance of
aerodynamic optimization and demonstrated a 6% improvement in stage efficiency for the redesigned
turbines compared to the original design.While the use case of cold flows for our design is the
confirmation of system stability, it is shown that cold flows can be used as a valid way to equate to
high-heat test cases. These results underscore the value of cold flow tests in refining turbine geometry and
ensuring alignment with design objectives, validating their suitability as a precursor to hot-fire testing.
However, a large challenge in testing or simulating RDE exhaust flows is the turbulence and unsteady
flow caused by a rotating detonation wave. It has been shown how nozzle guide vanes effectively
attenuate unsteady dynamic pressures by up to 60% and reduce stagnation pressure losses by 4%,
reducing the effects posed by the RDE’s high-pressure, unsteady exhaust. Confirming the function of the

CFD Tests of Loading: The goal of this test is to determine the maximum mechanical load experienced
by the system downstream of the RDE. Each section of the system (the shaft, the turbine blades, the
conical deflector, and the RDE inner and outer walls) must be able to withstand mechanical stress and
loading while the system is operating. The forces felt by these objects will come directly from the axial
rotation of the turbine and the resistance of the fluid flow through the system, in addition to simple
thermal expansion. A fully detailed CAD model will be made of the entire system, after which it will be
tested extensively in Ansys Workbench or other fluid dynamic simulation softwares. The primary goal of
the tests will be to accurately simulate the conditions seen by the objects while under operation. In the
case of Ansys, for example, a solution will be achieved when the scaled residuals reach a nominal value,
usually 1.00 e-3. This would indicate that the simulation is accurate, at which point we can extract the
maximum stress seen by the objects in the system.
Balance Test of Turbine: The turbine must remain balanced in order to prevent any resonance modes
from being reached when spinning at high RPMs. For these tests, the turbine will be placed on a
balancing machine and spun. Using the integrated software with the machine, we will be able to see if
there are imbalances within the rotor. If any imbalances are detected, the software automatically marks the
general area to remove the material. We will then again perform the tests until the software deems the
rotor balanced.
Loading Test of Turbine: A supersonic nozzle will be placed downstream of the turbine. In order to
ensure that the turbine will withstand an overspeed event, the flow will be imparted at a much higher
velocity and the turbine will be spun until the time reaches 30 seconds. The tests will then conclude, flow
will be stopped, and the turbine will be brought to rest. A visual inspection of the turbine will be
performed to ensure no deformations or cracks originated from the test.

Loading Test of Shaft: The shaft will be assembled per Fig. # (shaft assembly figure) and coupled to the
motor. Two tests shall be performed to ensure the security of the shaft. The first, being a static loading
test, in which additional loading will be applied to the turbine end to ensure a large safety factor (around
2-3). Next is a dynamic loading test, in which the motor slowly spins up the shaft to around 50,000 RPM,
and ensures that no resonance occurs. The test should be repeated to add additional loading to the end of
the shaft. The test shall be evaluated by visual inspection for any cracks and deformation. These tests will
occur at MAE West.
Loading Test of Motor: The function of the motor is to spin up the shaft and turbine to operating speeds
and subsequently generate electricity under RDC power. The loading of the motor during startup is
relatively low given the fact that the speeds can be controlled for a gradual climb to operating speeds. The
greatest loading on the motor occurs during power generation during which the RDC exhaust must
balance out the large slowing torques created by the braking resistors as they dissipate energy. During this
step of the procedure. Speeds remain the same as at startup but torques increase greatly. Therefore the
motor must be tested electrically and mechanically to ensure it can withstand this loading. This can be
achieved by loading the motor while the shaft is stalled. The objective of the test would be to achieve
powers exceeding what would be seen during power generation mode.
Loading Test of Cowling: The function of the cowling is to hold, with tight tolerances, the rotor blades
such that there is room for them to rotate without friction but with a minimal gap to prevent air bleeding
over the edges of the blades. The loads on the cowling involve pressure and thermal loads from the hot
exhaust and in the event of catastrophic failure, the blades. Besides computational analysis of the
component and the understanding that a stainless steel cylinder of 0.4 inches thick is capable of
withstanding a point force of 300 J, the material will be suitably tested to confirm its structural integrity.
This will be accomplished with the application of a force of 300 J or higher; a small object of appropriate
mass will be dropped onto the cowling. The cowling will pass the test as long as there is no visible
damage to it.
Loading Test of Safety Cowling: The safety cowling, made of Kevlar, serves as a last resort to prevent
any escaped fragments not captured by the cowling during the event of a catastrophic failure. To fulfil the
requirements of this test, the geometry of the cowling will be tested such that any fragments ejected from
the test article will be directed, or caught, to prevent further damage to property. The safety cowling will
be tested by supporting it horizontal to the ground, and dropping a small object of appropriate mass onto
it. The safety cowling will pass the loading test as long as it stops the motion of the object without
damage.

done to the EGU. This will be done by running the RDE at maximum allowable fuel flow for the
maximum time determined by other tests and measuring the temperature of the deflector both on the outer
surface and inner surface. This will give a temperature profile of the conical deflector and allow us to

as well as that the motor slows to a stop as expected when in power generation mode and not under RDC
power.

Instead of exhaust from the RDE, high pressure air will be flowed through the turbine that will create a
net power generation from the generator. We plan to use this test to validate all turbine related systems
with a functioning turbine before adding the hot exhaust from the RDC. Instrumentation throughout the
system will be tested during the cold flow as well. Goals of the test will include verifying mounting of the
turbine with the air flow and a test of RPM sensors to ensure accurate readings.
Cold Flow Test of Full System: A critical element of the testing process is the complete low-heat testing
of the entire system and its function. Cold flow tests are a standard procedure for all high stress engine
related test articles. A cold flow will be accomplished using compressed air injected directly into the
system in the same manner as an RDE's exhaust. This will allow for the full walk through of a full test run
check-list, starting from pre-spooling to power generation, to power storage or a visible representation of
power extraction. Testing the system in this way before the final test using high-heat exhaust ensures
proper tolerances are met that may result in catastrophic failure.

VII.F Summary of Previous Work
The development of this RDE-powered turbine generator system has been a highly iterative and
interdisciplinary process, marked by numerous design evolutions, engineering challenges, and
collaborative problem-solving. From the project's inception, our objective was clear: design a compact,
efficient system capable of extracting mechanical and electrical power from the extreme exhaust
environment of a rotating detonation combustor (RDC). This required not only understanding the unique
physics of detonation-based propulsion but also engineering a turbine, shaft assembly, starter-generator
system, and instrumentation setup robust enough to handle the associated thermal and dynamic loads. The
project began with extensive research into turbomachinery operating under high-frequency, unsteady
conditions. Our team explored several configurations for the turbine section, ultimately selecting a
high-speed axial design. Early CAD models were iterated upon in parallel with CFD simulations, which
informed decisions on blade profiles, flow conditioning, and housing geometry. Simultaneously,
mechanical analyses such as modal testing of the shaft assembly ensured our design would maintain
structural integrity at target speeds exceeding 30,000 RPM. As design decisions matured, our attention
shifted toward fabrication and systems integration. Partnering with facilities like CAMAL and BE-FAST
Lab, we machined precision components such as the conical deflector, turbine housing, and shaft mounts.
Instrumentation and safety systems—including custom LabVIEW code for abort protocols—were
integrated to support real-time monitoring and risk mitigation during testing. Despite several redesigns
prompted by material limitations, time constraints, and system interference, the team adapted quickly.
Budget overages were handled strategically, with the majority of the costs concentrated in the turbine and
starter-generator subsystems, justified by their complexity and performance requirements. Ultimately,
each subsystem—fluid, mechanical, and electrical—was designed not just to function in isolation, but to
operate as a cohesive, safe, and testable platform for future RDE turbine-generator development. This
foundation paves the way for more advanced iterations, longer runtime tests, and expanded academic and
industry collaboration.

X. Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate and validate the feasibility of integrating a RDC with a
turbine generator for efficient power generation under the demanding conditions of supersonic exhaust
flows. Throughout the design, analysis, fabrication, and testing stages, the team faced and overcame a
variety of interdisciplinary engineering challenges, each critical to the functionality and safety of the
overall system. Significant accomplishments include the successful development of a high-speed
supersonic axial turbine utilizing comprehensive aerodynamic analyses with 0-D and 1-D modeling
approaches, combined with detailed CFD simulations to refine blade geometry and mitigate aerodynamic
losses from shock interactions. Further design complexities were addressed in the shaft and bearing
assembly, where meticulous modal analyses ensured that resonant frequencies would not compromise
structural integrity during high-speed operation. Pre-lubricated bearings were ultimately selected for their
simplicity and efficacy within the short operational timeframe specified, significantly reducing the
complexity and fabrication time of the lubrication subsystem. The integration of the starter-generator
subsystem further expanded the project's multidisciplinary nature, necessitating detailed electrical and
mechanical modeling within Simulink to achieve proper torque and power matching. A robust control
architecture was realized through a custom LabVIEW VI, ensuring remote operability, real-time
monitoring, and automatic safety abort logic, thus safeguarding both the hardware and personnel
