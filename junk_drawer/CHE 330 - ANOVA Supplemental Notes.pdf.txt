Hypothesis Testing II: F-Test / Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)†

Fall 2011

The purpose of an analysis of variance or ANOVA is to find out whether data from several
population groups of interest have a common mean, i.e., to determine whether the groups share a
common measured characteristic or are, in fact, different in the measured characteristic. Thus,
ANOVA is used to determine if there is any difference between the means of two or more
population groups. If there are only two population groups, one can employ the simple
hypothesis testing approach to determine if there is a statistical difference between the two
means. When there are more than two population groups with more than two means to compare,
one could attempt multiple pair-wise comparisons. Thus, for three population groups, A, B, and
C, such an approach would yield three binary comparisons, namely, A-B, A-C, B-C. Likewise,
for four population groups, A, B, C, and D, six binary comparisons would result, namely, A-B, AC, A-D, B-C, B-D, and C-D. The number of binary comparisons increases rapidly with the
number of population groups. The approach is inefficient and, more importantly, it suffers from
two serious drawbacks that make the results less than reliable. The first drawback is an
increased likelihood of the so-called Type I error. Recall that in a standard hypothesis test, the
significance level is the probability that one might accidentally reject a true null hypothesis.
When the number of binary comparisons increases, the chances of these Type I errors increases
proportionally. The second drawback is a failure to account for possible interaction effects that
cannot be detected or analyzed in a simple hypothesis test (see later).

Solution
At first glance, the data seems to suggest that the bacteria count in milk is a function of delivery
tanker. We need to confirm to confirm the suspicion with an ANOVA analysis.
First, postulate the Null Hypothesis that the above data merely reflects normal scatters in data
and that there is no difference in the sample means of bacteria counts with tankers.
Mathematically, the Null Hypothesis may be formulated as
Ho: <Y1,j> = <Y2,j> = ...= <Y5,j>
The Alternate Hypothesis may then be formulated as Ha: <Y1,j> ≠ <Y2,j> ≠ … ≠ <Y5,j>
ANOVA provides a prescribed protocol to validate or reject the Null Hypothesis. The procedure
is as follows (see later for a simpler procedure):
(1) Calculate the Level mean of each group of data for each level of the factor (in this case, tanker)
<YI,j> = [YI,1 + YI,2 + …. YI,nj]/NI where NI ≡ number of data points corresponding to the ith
level. Results are shown in the table on next page.
(2) Calculate the Grand or Overall mean of ALL data
<Y> = [∑(∑YI,j)j]I/NTotal = [∑(∑YI,j)j]I/[∑NI]I
Note: Grand Mean = Average of Level Means.
(3) Calculate the Total Sum of Squares of Differences of ALL data points.
SSDT ≡ {∑[∑(YI,j - <Y>)2]j}I = [∑(∑YI,j2)j]I - NTotal<Y>2. Results of Steps 2 & 3 are
shown in the table on the page after next.
(4) Calculate the Sum of Squares of Differences Due to Different Levels of a Factor (which
measures the variability due to factors) SSDL ≡ ∑[NI<YI,j>2]I - NTotal<Y>2
(5) Calculate the Sum of Squares of Differences Due to Random Errors (which measures the
variability due to unexplained factors)
SSDE ≡ SSDT – SSDL = ∑[(NI − 1)St.Dev.I2]I
(6) Calculate the Degrees of Freedom for the Total Data Points (dfT), for the Levels of a factor
(dfL), and for Random Errors (dfE) as follows: dfT = NTotal − 1, dfL = NL − 1, dfE = NTotal – NL
where NL is the total number of levels for a factor.
(7) Calculate the Mean Square Value for Different Levels of a factor and for Errors as defined
below: MSL ≡ SSDL/dfL = SSDL/(NL − 1), MSE ≡ SSDE/dfE = SSDE/(NTotal – NL)
(8) Calculate the F-value for the Null Hypothesis as follows, F = MSL/MSE
(9) Look up in an F-table the critical F-value for the specified significance level and degrees of
freedom for the factors and errors, Fcritical = Fα ,dfL ,dfE
(10) Finally, compare F with Fcritical to make one of the following conclusions:
If F > Fcritical, Ho may be rejected and Ha accepted at the specified significance level.
If F < Fcritical, Ho may NOT be rejected and one must conclude that there is insufficient
evidence to accept Ha at the specified significance level.

Alternate, Simpler Hand-Calculation Solution
A simpler ANOVA procedure to validate or reject the Null Hypothesis is as follows:
(1) Set up the table of data with data entries on the various levels for the factor.
(2) Compute the mean of data within each level (Level Mean, LM) using the average (__ : __)
feature on Excel.
(3) Compute the variance within each level (Level Variance, sL2) using the relation, Level
Variance sL2 = (Level Standard Deviation sL )2 = (stdev(__ : __))^2 feature on Excel.
(4) Compute MSE by setting it equal to the average of the level variances, i.e.,

(6) Compute MSL by setting it equal nIsLM2, i.e., MSL = nIsLM2, where nI is the number of data

(7) Then compute F = MSL/MSE and compare it against Fc corresponding to the specified
