C.​ Atomic force microscopy. Contact mode

DVD:
1.​ Data.
AFM imaging of DVDs was performed in air during the optimization of experimental conditions. All
measurements were carried out in Static Force mode using a Stat0.2LAuD cantilever with a set point force of
20 nN. To optimize the imaging conditions for surface analysis of the DVD sample, several AFM scans were
acquired while systematically adjusting the feedback parameters, scan size, and image orientation. An initial
image (Figure 1) was recorded with a scan size of 15 µm using default feedback settings (P-gain = 10,000,
I-gain = 1750). Although the periodic surface tracks were visible, the image suffered from limited lateral
resolution and unstable contrast, indicating non-ideal PID tuning and the presence of tip–sample interaction
artifacts.
In a subsequent scan (Figure 2), the same scan size was maintained while significantly increasing the
proportional gain (P-gain = 30,000). This adjustment led to a noticeable improvement in image stability and
definition of the periodic features. In addition, a rotation of 90° was applied to align the fast scan direction
with the orientation of the surface tracks, resulting in sharper lines and reduced noise.
It should be noted that excessively high feedback gain can also lead to increased image noise due to
feedback-induced oscillations, as the controller may overreact to rapid height variations. Therefore, image
quality does not improve monotonically with increasing gain, and an optimal gain range must be identified to
balance tracking accuracy and feedback stability.
To further improve measurement accuracy and minimize drift effects, the scan area was reduced to 5 µm and
repositioned over a flatter region of the sample (Figure 3). More moderate feedback parameters (P-gain =
15,000, I-gain = 1500) were selected to ensure stable tracking of the surface topography. The final image
(Figure 4), acquired with a rotation angle of 160°, provided a stable, high-resolution topography in which
individual grooves and reliefs were clearly distinguishable. This image was selected for subsequent
quantitative analysis, including the determination of interline distance, groove depth, and relief thickness.

9

2.​ Image analysis
An AFM line profile was extracted from the optimized DVD surface image and converted to nanometer-scale
units. To remove global tilt and facilitate feature identification, the height data were linearly detrended. The
interline distance was estimated by identifying local minima in the smoothed profile, corresponding to the
centers of adjacent grooves. The distances between consecutive minima were calculated and averaged,
yielding an interline spacing of approximately 743 nm, consistent with the nominal DVD track pitch.
The lateral thickness of the reliefs (lands) was determined by defining a height threshold at the midpoint
between groove and land levels in the flattened profile. The positions at which the profile crossed this
threshold were used to estimate the lateral extent of each relief, and the resulting values were averaged,
giving a mean relief thickness of approximately 260 nm. The average relief height was obtained from the
peak-to-valley differences measured along the profile, resulting in a value of approximately 19.8 nm.

Erithrocytes:
1.​ Data
For the Erythrocyte sample, Images were acquired at different scan sizes and different gain parameters to
balance resolution and statistical representativity.
A large-area image with a scan size of 50 µm × 50 µm (Figure 1) was obtained using feedback gains P-gain =
10,000 and I-gain = 1000, a scan time of approximately 1 s per line, and a resolution of 50 × 50 points. This
image provided a sufficiently wide field of view to observe several well-separated erythrocytes and was
therefore selected for quantitative size analysis.
A second image with a smaller scan size of 15 µm × 15 µm (Figure 2) was acquired using the same feedback
gains but with a higher spatial sampling (256 × 256 points). Despite the increased nominal resolution, this
image exhibited a higher level of noise and pronounced scan-line artifacts. This behavior is attributed to the
increased sensitivity of static-force operation at small scan sizes, where longer effective interaction times per
surface feature enhance the influence of thermal drift and mechanical vibrations. Consequently, this image
was used only during the optimization phase and not for quantitative measurements.
It should be noted that complementary measurements in Dynamic AFM mode were planned; however, due
to technical issues with the dynamic module, these measurements could not be completed. As a result, the
analysis was based on a limited number of static-force images with moderate overall quality.

2.​ Image analysis
Quantitative image analysis was performed on the 50 µm × 50 µm AFM image of erythrocytes. After
calibration and plane leveling in Gwyddion, the lateral size of individual cells was determined using the
distance measurement tool. The edge-to-edge diameters of five well-isolated erythrocytes were measured
manually to ensure consistent identification of cell boundaries.
The measured diameters were exported to Excel and averaged, yielding an average erythrocyte diameter of
approximately 8.2 µm. The obtained value is consistent with typical erythrocyte diameters reported in the
literature.

TGT1:
1.​ Data
AFM measurements were conducted on the TGT1 calibration grating, consisting of a periodic array of sharp
silicon peaks. Multiple images were acquired to optimize the imaging conditions for reliable quantitative
analysis.
An initial image with a scan size of 15 µm × 15 µm (Figure 1) was recorded using feedback gains P = 5000
and I = 1500, with the scan direction set to Up and a rotation angle of 160°. This image exhibited pronounced
scan-line artifacts and asymmetric peak shapes. The relatively low proportional gain, combined with the large
rotation angle and static-force operation, resulted in an insufficient feedback response to the steep surface
features of the grating, rendering the image unsuitable for quantitative analysis.
A second image with the same scan size (Figure 2) was acquired using optimized feedback parameters (P =
10,000, I = 1000), a Down scan direction, and a rotation angle of 90°. Under these conditions, the periodic
peak array was clearly resolved, with improved symmetry and significantly reduced scan-line noise. This
image represented a good compromise between field of view and resolution and was therefore selected for
the quantitative determination of the lattice parameter and average peak height.
An additional image with a smaller scan size of 5 µm × 5 µm (Figure 3) was acquired using a higher integral
gain (I = 2000) to enhance local detail. Although this image provided improved visualization of individual
peaks, the reduced scan area limited statistical representativity. For this reason, it was not used for primary
quantitative analysis.

13

2.​ Image analysis
Quantitative analysis was performed on the optimized AFM image of the TGT1 calibration grating. After
calibration, the image was plane-leveled to remove background tilt and minimize long-range height
variations. Line profiles were then extracted along a principal lattice direction, identified from the visual
alignment of the periodic peak array, in order to probe the characteristic spatial periodicity of the surface.
Local minima were determined from the line profiles using a local-extremum criterion, requiring the height at
each point to be lower than its immediate neighbors, and these minima were used as reference positions for
spacing determination.
The lattice parameter was obtained by measuring nearest-neighbor peak-to-peak distances along two parallel
line profiles extracted from different regions of the image. The individual distances from both profiles were
14

combined and averaged to improve statistical representativeness, yielding an average lattice spacing of
approximately 2.14 µm, consistent with the expected periodicity of the TGT1 structure.
To estimate the average peak height, the same line profiles were analyzed by identifying local maxima
corresponding to the peak apices and local minima corresponding to the valleys between adjacent peaks. The
peak height for each period was defined as the vertical difference between a peak maximum and the
adjacent local minimum. The resulting peak-to-valley heights were averaged, giving an average peak height of
approximately 0.38 µm.
Both the lattice parameter and the peak height obtained from this analysis are in agreement with reported
specifications for TGT1 calibration gratings. Small deviations are attributed to tip–sample convolution effects,
the finite lateral resolution of the measurement, and the one-dimensional nature of the line-profile-based
analysis.

Nanoholes:
1.​ Data
Several images of nanoholes were acquired at different scan sizes and resolutions to assess the overall
surface morphology and to provide a qualitative comparison with images obtained in non-contact AFM
mode.
Two images were recorded with a scan size of 15 µm × 15 µm under similar scanning conditions, using a
setpoint force of 20 nN and relatively high proportional gains (P-gain = 25,000 and P-gain = 20,000,
respectively), with I-gain fixed at 1500. These large-area scans allow visualization of the spatial distribution
and uniformity of the nanohole pattern, but exhibit scan-line artifacts and contrast instabilities associated
with static-force operation and high feedback gain.
An additional image was acquired at a smaller scan size of 5 µm × 5 µm using reduced feedback gains (P-gain
= 10,000, I-gain = 1000) to improve local tracking of the surface topography . This image provides enhanced
detail of individual nanohole features, although residual distortions remain due to feedback limitations.
No quantitative image analysis was performed on the nanohole sample. The acquired AFM images are
included exclusively for qualitative comparison with non-contact AFM measurements, highlighting
differences in contrast, stability, and feature definition between contact and non-contact imaging modes.

16

A comparison between static (contact) and dynamic (non-contact) AFM imaging of the nanohole sample
highlights the advantages of dynamic mode for this system. While static AFM images show the overall
periodic structure, they exhibit increased scan-line artifacts, distortions, and less uniform contrast,
particularly at larger scan sizes. In contrast, dynamic AFM provides improved image stability, clearer
definition of individual nanoholes, and more reliable depth contrast, owing to the reduced lateral forces and
minimized tip–sample interaction. As a result, dynamic AFM is better suited for quantitative analysis of
nanostructured surfaces such as nanohole arrays.

Conclusions
Scanning Tunneling Microscopy enabled the atomic-scale characterization of the graphite surface through
progressive reduction of the scan size and optimization of feedback parameters. While large-area images
were used to identify defect-free regions, smaller scans revealed the characteristic electronic contrast of the
graphite lattice, with improved hexagonal symmetry observed after reducing the feedback gain. Fourier
analysis of multiple line profiles extracted from the optimized image yielded a lattice parameter of 0.214 nm,
which is in reasonable agreement with the reference value for graphite. Minor deviations are attributed to
feedback-induced distortions and the intrinsic sensitivity of STM to electronic rather than purely geometric
structure.
Dynamic (non-contact) AFM enabled stable imaging and quantitative characterization of the nanohole array.
Analysis of multiple line profiles allowed the determination of the nanohole depth and lattice parameter,
yielding values of 0.12 ± 0.01 µm and 0.89 ± 0.07 µm, respectively. These results confirm the suitability of
non-contact AFM for reliable measurement of depth and periodicity in nanostructured surfaces.
Contact mode AFM was successfully employed to characterize four distinct samples with different surface
morphologies and length scales: a DVD surface, erythrocytes, TGT1 calibration grating and Nano holes. For
the DVD sample, the extracted interline distance and relief dimensions were found to be in good agreement
17

with the nominal DVD track pitch and reported structural parameters, confirming the reliability of the
imaging and analysis procedure. The erythrocyte measurements yielded an average cell diameter consistent
with literature values for red blood cells, demonstrating the capability of AFM to resolve micrometer-scale
biological structures despite the inherent limitations of static-force imaging.
For the TGT1 calibration grating, both the lattice parameter and peak height were determined from
optimized AFM images and line-profile analysis. The measured peak height falls within the nominal range
specified for the grating, while the lattice spacing shows reasonable agreement with the reference value
when considering the one-dimensional nature of the analysis and potential projection effects. Overall, the
results highlight the importance of careful optimization of feedback parameters and scan conditions in AFM
measurements and demonstrate that, when properly tuned, AFM provides quantitative and reliable surface
characterization across a wide range of sample types.
