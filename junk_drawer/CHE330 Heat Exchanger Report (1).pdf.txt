Abstract
The Double-Pipe Heat Exchanger experiment analyzes heat transfer properties in
co-current and counter-current heat exchangers. In this experiment, heat is exchanged between
the wall separating hot water flowing in the inner tube and cold water flowing through the outer
tube of the exchanger. Heat transfer data was collected for a combination of hot and cold flow
rates varying from 4.109 to 7.997 L/min for both co-current and counter-current streams. These
flow rates correspond to reflux ratios of 4 to 7. The highest heat transfer rate was found to be
5700 ± 200 W in co-current mode, and a rate of 6100 ± 200 W in counter-current mode, both of
which were observed at a cold stream flow rate of 7.7 ± 0.5 L/min. Therefore, in both modes, to
get a higher heat transfer rate, the most important factor is to increase the cold water flow rate. A
higher cold water flow rate thins the boundary layer and lowers the convective heat transfer
resistance of the stream, allowing for a higher heat transfer rate. When comparing results from
the two modes, it was found that the counter-current heat exchanger more efficiently transferred
heat, as seen by the 400 W difference between the two mode’s highest heat transfer rate. Data for
this experiment also supports that a lower heat resistance, a larger heat transfer coefficient, and a
larger temperature difference are important factors in increasing the heat transfer rate.

Introduction
Heat transfer is an important concept in chemical engineering theory and is an important
step in many industrial processes. The three types of heat transfer are conduction, convection,
and radiation. Conduction is the heat transfer that occurs between direct contact of matter with a
solid object. Convection is the heat transfer that occurs due to the movement of fluids. The
driving force for free convection is a density gradient, caused by a temperature gradient, and the
driving force for forced convection is a pressure gradient. Radiation is heat transfer through
electromagnetic waves [1].
Heat exchangers utilize these heat transfer methods and are used in a variety of industrial
processes, including energy production, refrigeration, oil refinery, manufacturing, etc. [2]. The
double-pipe heat exchanger, though not the most commonly used heat exchanger in industry, is
useful as it allows a simple approach for understanding conductive and convective heat transfer
principles. In this experiment, the effects of volumetric flow rate, temperature gradient, and heat
exchanger configuration on the overall heat transfer will be investigated [1].

conducted under both conditions. Hot water flowed through the inner tube while cold water was
fed through the outer tube from a chilled water reservoir. The flow rates of both the hot and cold

and recording the data, care was taken to establish that the flows of the streams were turbulent in

Theoretically, the heat transfer rates Qhot and Qcold should be equal, but within the laboratory
setting the average of the two being less than 15% qualifies as the system reaching steady state.
This typically took between 30-60 seconds, however, if the temperature value readings did not
stabilize within that time frame the data was only recorded after they stabilized. [1]
For both co-current and counter-current trials, 16 experimental runs were conducted and
the hot and cold streams were varied under a 4x4 combination. The same hot flow rate was used
for 4 runs while changing the cold flow rate 4 times. Next, the hot flow rate was changed and
kept the same for the next 4 runs, and once again the cold flow rate was changed 4 times. This
was repeated for a total of 4 different hot flow rates with each also under 4 different cold cold
flow rates. This same experimental procedure was used for both co-current and counter-current
trials. For the 4 different flow rates, both hot and cold streams used the conditions of a reflux
ratio of 4, 5, 6, and 7. This translates to flow rates 4.1, 5.4, 6.7, and 7.9 L/min for the hot stream,
and 4.6, 5.6, 6.6, and 7.7 L/min for the cold stream. For each trial, other than flow rates, the
temperatures of the hot and cold streams at the inlet, outlet, and midpoint of the pipe were
measured. [1]

Results and Discussion
Heat exchangers are restricted by how well they can overcome heat transfer resistances
within their system. In addition, another factor that affects the effectiveness of a heat exchanger
is the difference in temperature of the two entering streams of fluid. A larger temperature
difference results in a more efficient heat transfer rate. A lower overall heat transfer resistance
results in a higher heat transfer coefficient and more importantly, a higher heat transfer rate. A
higher heat transfer rate signifies a smaller necessary heat exchange area and thus reduces the
cost needed to construct a heat exchanger. The heat transfer resistances that affect a heat
exchanger are convective, conductive, and scale.
For each system, 1/UA and (1/Vc)^0.8 were calculated using Equations 11 and 12.
While the meaning of these graphs is easily obscured behind calculations, a simple analysis
showed that in trials with a constant flow of hot water, increasing the flow of cold water will
increase the heat transferred between flows. This is shown in Figures 3-6. As explained in the
theory section, 1/UA is the total heat transfer resistance between the hot and cold streams and
1/Vc^0.8 is understood to be a function of the flow velocity. As the flow velocity increases, so

and Figure 4 shown below, it is apparent that the highest total heat transfer resistance happened

Another factor that affects the effectiveness of a heat exchanger is the difference between
the temperatures of the hot and cold fluids. A larger difference in temperature leads to a greater
driving force in the transfer of heat. Figure 7 shows the temperature profile for the co-current
system. At L=0, both hot water and cold water streams are entering, and the temperature
difference between them is at a maximum. As the fluid travels along the length of the pipe L, the
temperature difference between them decreases, until they exit the system at L=3. Because they
are co-current, in order for the hot fluid to reach the temperature of the cold fluid the length of
the pipe would need to be infinite. Because of this, co-current heat exchangers are known to be
the less efficient way of cooling fluids. Despite this, co-current heat exchangers are still used in
industrial applications for a variety of reasons.

Conclusion
Heat exchangers addressed how to examine and calculate the heat transfer and heat
resistance of a double-pipe, single-pass heat exchanger in both co-current and counter-current
modes. Within the double pipe, cold water flowed through the outer tube and hot water flowed
through the inner tube and their temperatures were measured using six thermistors located at the
inlet, mid-point, and outlet of the heat exchanger.
In co-current mode, the highest heat transfer rate was 5700 ± 200 W observed at a cold
stream flow rate of 7.7 ± 0.5 L/min and velocity of 1.5 ± 0.1 m/s and a hot stream flow rate of
5.4 ± 0.3 L/min and velocity of 0.92 ± 0.04 m/s. There was a log mean temperature difference
18

between the streams of 25.05 ± 0.05. Average heat transfer resistances of the trials included a
convective heat transfer resistance in the hot stream of 0.00176 ± 0.00009 K/W, a convective
heat transfer resistance in the cold stream of 0.0028 ± 0.0001 K/W, a total scale resistance of
-0.00005 ± 0.00008 K/W, and a conductive heat transfer resistance of 0.00038 K/W.
In counter-current mode, the highest heat transfer rate was 6100 ± 200 W observed at a
cold stream flow rate of 7.7 ± 0.5 L/min and velocity of 1.5 ± 0.1 m/s and a hot stream flow rate
of 8.00 ± 0.3 L/min and velocity of 1.36 ± 0.04 m/s. There was a log mean temperature
difference between the streams of 24.03 ± 0.05. Average heat transfer resistances of the trials
included a convective heat transfer resistance in the hot stream of 0.00158 ± 0.00008 K/W, a
convective heat transfer resistance in the cold stream of 0.0029 ± 0.0001 K/W, a total scale
resistance of 0 ± 0.00008 K/W, and a conductive heat transfer resistance of 0.00038 K/W.
Based on these results, counter-current experienced a higher heat transfer rate than
co-current which means for a heat exchanger, using a counter-current mode is more effective in
transferring heat. In addition, most of the heat transfer resistance is in the convective of the cold
stream for both modes. For that reason, to improve heat transfer focus needs to be on increasing
the flow rate of the cold stream to thin the boundary layer and thus reduce the convective heat
transfer resistance of the stream.
The key takeaways from this experiment are that a counter-current heat exchanger is
more efficient than a co-current heat exchanger and when designing a heat exchanger, factors
such as heat resistance, heat transfer coefficient, and temperature difference of the two streams
are important. The trials with the highest heat transfer didn’t have the highest temperature
difference of all the trials. Even so, there is still a vague relationship between the two. A lower
heat resistance and consequently a larger heat transfer coefficient and a larger temperature

19

difference result in a larger heat transfer rate. A larger heat transfer rate means less area needed
for heat transfer and therefore less cost for the construction of a heat exchanger.

Recommendations
For this experiment, there are no recommendations for improvements on the physical
experiment. There were large errors in the velocities of the streams, but that requires either a
better rotameter or a more effective way to measure flow rate which would require a larger
budget than how much of an impact the change would have. In terms of instructions, the data
analysis was fairly vague and could have been explained better, there were several calculations
that needed to be done and thought through in order to understand the graphs properly, leading to
much head scratching and many questions sent to friends and professors.
