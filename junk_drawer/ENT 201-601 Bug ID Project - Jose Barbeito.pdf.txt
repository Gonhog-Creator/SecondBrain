Written Summary on Glossina Tachinoides, the Tsetse fly
11/7/23
Glossina Tachinoides is a species of Tsetse fly. The Tsetse fly lives in sub-Saharan africa
and is a blood feeding insect, feeding primarily on wild hogs, domestic cattle, humans, and other
mammals. During my research, I was shocked to find the wide variety of sources of blood for
this little fly. With around 25 different species, a wide variety of hosts have been found for this
fly, including warthogs, giraffe, hippopotamus’s, buffalo and kudu, aardvarks and porcupines,
crocodiles, hundreds of species of birds, rhinos, snakes, elephants, ostriches, and of course,
humans. The wide variety of food sources leads to a worrying source of disease, since many
Glossina flies, but especially Tachinoides, carry the parasite responsible for African Sleeping
Sickness, a trypanosomiasis parasite. This parasite lives in the liver, heart, and kidney of
mammals and can spread to the brain, causing coma and death.
The African variant is aptly named Sleeping Sickness due to this coma-inducing
parasite, with around 700 cases being reported in 2022. The number of infections, while low
today, has dropped significantly since 1999, where around 28,000 cases were reported. There
could be many causes to this, but awareness and proper prevention is most likely the cause.
The life cycle of a Tsetse fly is peculiar and noteworthy. The mother tsetse flies begins by
producing an egg inside her uterus, which grows over the course of 10 days, going through the
three larval instar stages. The offspring are fed a milky substance created by the mother.
Because of this, the mother is a voracious feeder during pregnancy, because she has to feed
not only herself but the offspring she carries. At the end of the third instar, the larva crawls out of
the mother. At this point, the larva is almost the size of the mother, leading to a scenic birth. The
larva, once outside, will harden its shell and complete its metamorphosis. The mother gives birth
every 10 days, leading to a slow and linear growth curve. This is quite opposite to normal flies,
who lay many eggs on a substrate and leave them to fend for themselves.
I found many other interesting facts about the Tsetse fly while doing my research for the
presentation. Along with its spectacular reproductive cycle, the Glossina Tachinoides has been
targeted for control in livestock, primarily because of its many disease vectors, but specifically
sleeping sickness. In animals, sleeping sickness is called Nagana. Ancient control techniques
include clearing land, slaughtering wild animals, and pesticides, all of which are ecologically
disastrous. More recent control techniques include direct trapping of the flies and sterilization of
mass produced males, which are then released into the environment. Both methods have had
success, and are being implemented in cattle and livestock farms in Africa today. To trap the
flies, scientists and farmers use chemical attractants, including acetone, carbon dioxide, and
octenol. Cheaper attractants are usually used for mass trapping, the primary source of which is
cow urine. Lastly, another fun fact about the fly is that there is a theory that the stripes on
Zebras are meant to confuse Tsetse flies, not for camouflage, as black and white alternating
bands have been shown to be confusing for the flies, who prefer darker hide such as those of
cows and buffalo.
In conclusion, the Tsetse fly is an annoying, disease driving blood feeder in Africa that
feeds on a wide variety of animals, yet its unique reproductive cycle makes it deserving of a
closer look.
