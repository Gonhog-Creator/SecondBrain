ABSTRACT

A fast model of a Rotating Detonation Combustor (RDC) is developed based on the Method
of Characteristics (MOC). The model provides a CFD-like solution of an unwrapped 2D RDC
flow field in under 10 seconds with similar fidelity as 2D Reacting URANS simulations.
Parametric studies are conducted using the simplified model, and the trends are analyzed to gain
insight into the underlying physics of rotating detonation combustors. A methodology to assess
the performance of operation with multiple waves is presented. The main effect of increasing
waves is found to be the increase in the exit Mach number of the combustion chamber. The design
process of a mixer component is also presented. The mixer lies downstream of a channel-cooled
RDC with subsonic exit and upstream of a Rolls-Royce M250 helicopter engine in open-loop
configuration. The mixer dilutes the RDC exhaust with approximately 250% air to condition the
flow for the M250 turbine at steady state operation, while also acting as an isolator with a choked
throat to prevent back propagation of pressure waves. The mixer aerodynamic design was
completed using 2D axisymmetric RANS simulations, and the mechanical design was evaluated
using Ansys Mechanical FEA and was found to be able to survive the high thermal stresses present
both during the transient heating and steady state operating condition.

INTRODUCTION

Rotating detonation is a promising technology to enhance the performance of conventional
gas turbine engines by increasing combustion efficiency and reducing the weight and size of the
combustor. Deflagration is the typical method by which heat is added to a combustion engine,
however this process is less thermodynamically efficient than detonation. While deflagration is
approximately isobaric combustion, detonation is a form of pressure-gain combustion. The
pressure rise across a detonation wave may be as much as 55 times the initial pressure [1], with
predicted fuel consumption savings of up to 9% [2]. Due to the high reaction speed, the heat release
occurs over a very small region, allowing for very power-dense compact combustors [3]. Due to
the potential benefits of Rotating Detonation Combustors (RDCs), significant research effort has
been devoted to the numerical and experimental study of their performance and how they can be
applied to rocket and air-breathing jet engines [4] [5] [6]. Despite theoretical and numerical
predictions, many of these potential advantages are as of yet unrealized in experimental tests. This
is due to the inherent challenges associated with the fundamental operating physics of RDCs.
Rotating detonation engines are based on the phenomenon of detonation, in which a combined
shock wave and reaction zone cause a combustion reaction to occur in a very short temporal and
spatial span. The heat release of the reaction sustains the forward propagation of the detonation
wave which may travel at several kilometers per second. In an RDC, one or more of these
detonation waves are confined in an annular or hollow cylindrical combustor and propagate
azimuthally around the wall(s). Due to the high heat release over a short region, the heat transfer
in RDCs necessitates advanced cooling techniques and high-strength alloys [7]. The injector
scheme is also a significant area of study, since injection of reactants often happens with large
pressure ratio into the combustor to prevent backflow. This stiffness of injection tends to lead to
very large losses which decrease the measured pressure gain ratio below unity [8]. Due to the
typically near stoichiometric combustion investigated, dilution of the products before downstream
components is necessary for sustained testing [7]. As a result of the high heat transfer and necessity
of dilution for longer duration tests, experimental investigation of RDCs is typically limited to
short duration tests under 5 seconds. The longest reported continuous test of an RDC was 6-10
seconds by Isihara in 2017 [9]. Lastly, the large-amplitude, high-frequency pressure fluctuations

14

of the RDC may interfere with work extraction by turbines downstream or cause accelerated
fatigue.
Due to the inherently unsteady and reacting nature of rotating detonation, numerical
evaluation of combustor geometry is very computationally expensive, and large-scale optimization
and parametric studies are infeasible. The fine mesh and minuscule time step required to accurately
model the detonation process mean that 3D Unsteady Reynolds-Averaged-Navier-Stokes
(URANS) simulations frequently take weeks or months to converge to a quasi-steady solution [10].
The computational intensity can be reduced by sacrificing accuracy and simulating rotating
detonation on a flat 2D plane with periodic walls. These 2D URANS simulations remove the
curvature of the combustor but can be completed in approximately hours or days. To reduce
computation time further, simplified models of rotating detonation have been developed. One of
the most promising concepts is based on the Method of Characteristics (MOC) and the Zel’Dovichvon Neumann-Döring (ZND) model of detonation. These models solve the detonation reaction
using the ZND model then use a 2D MOC solver to model the flow field of the rest of the RDC in
the frame of reference of the detonation. In this way, a steady solution may be acquired with the
detonation reaction confined to a small region in space, significantly reducing computation time
to a matter of minutes. Several of these models exist [11] [12], although they remain under-utilized
for the analysis of RDC physics. In this thesis, a new model of a Rotating Detonation Combustor
based on the MOC is developed and investigated in detail to reveal the physical nature of rotating
detonation waves. Separate, but related to this objective; a mixer is aerodynamically and
mechanically designed to couple an RDC to conventional turbomachinery with dilution for future
experimental analysis at steady state. In designing this mixing component, the techniques for
aerodynamic and structural design are documented including a choked throat for isolating the RDC
from downstream influences and mechanical design for large axial gradients in temperature.
In Chapter 2 of this thesis, a fast 2D model of rotating detonation is developed. This model is
composed of two main parts: the ZND model of detonation and the 2D Method of Characteristics.
These constituent parts are studied individually to understand their effects on the results produced
by the model. These elements are then combined into an iterative model which can compute a
converged 2D solution of a RDC in approximately 10 seconds. The physics of this model are then
investigated and explained based on the underlying physics of the constituent parts. In addition, a

15

novel methodology for understanding co-rotating waves is proposed and analyzed using the 2D
model.
In Chapter 3 of this thesis, a mixing component is designed to connect an RDC to conventional
turbomachinery. This process begins with a 0D tool to predict the amount of dilution required to
decrease the average flow temperature to an acceptable level for the turbine. Another 0D tool is
developed to predict the change in mean flow angle associated with a change in mean radius and
flow area of the annular passage. Next, the contours of the mixer walls determined by a 2D
axisymmetric CFD study of several wall shapes. The length of the mixer and the location of a
choked throat inside are determined by a parametric study of the uniformity of the exit profiles of
Mach number and temperature. The optimized aerodynamic design of the mixer is then
mechanically designed to withstand steady state and transient thermal stresses, which were found
to be considerable.

MODEL OF ROTATING DETONATION

2.1

Fundamental Components of the Rotating Detonation Model

The two most important components of the RDC model are the ZND model of detonation
and the Method of Characteristics (MOC). The ZND model is a 1D model of a detonation which
predicts how the properties of a gas change across a detonation wave and predicts the speed of the
detonation wave. The MOC is a numerical solution method which solves the 2D flow field of a
supersonic region given some boundary conditions and known flow conditions at discrete points.

2.1.1

The ZND Model of Detonation
In the early 1940’s, Yakov Zel’Dovich, John von Neumann, and Werner Döring

independently proposed the same model for the structure of a detonation wave [13] [14] [15]. In
this simple model, known as the ZND model, a detonation wave consists of two parts. First, a
shock wave, modeled as an infinitesimally thin line extending normal to its direction of
propagation, compresses a gaseous mixture of reactants. Figure 1 depicts this process visually.
Across the moving shock wave, the pressure, temperature, and density of the gas increase
according to the Rankine-Hugoniot equations, initiating a combustion reaction. The heat released
by the reaction both propels the detonation forward, sustaining its propagation, and causes the flow
to choke according to Rayleigh flow theory in the frame of reference attached to the detonation
wave. The corresponding velocity of the wave is known as the Chapman-Jouget velocity [16].

Figure 1. Detonation wave structure

17

Figure 2 shows a P-v diagram of the thermodynamic process. As can be seen in the P-v diagram,
starting from the initial state of the stationary reactants, the flow is compressed by the shock and
the state of the unreacted gases travels along the shock Hugoniot curve until it reaches the von
Neumann peak. Behind the shock, the combustion reaction begins to release heat, moving the state
of the now reacting gases along the Rayleigh line of heat addition until the flow is choked in the
detonation frame of reference. This termination point is known as the Chapman Jouget Condition
[17].

Figure 2. P-v diagram of ZND detonation
The ZND model treats the detonation process as 1D planar, assuming changes in properties only
along the direction in which the detonation moves. Real detonations may not be planar and have
gradients in all directions. Additionally, real shocks are not infinitesimally thin, and at very small
scales the width of the shock can affect the structure of the detonation. For the purposes of
modeling rotating detonation in 2D, these effects can be neglected, as the shape of detonation
fronts predicted by 2D CFD are nearly linear, and the width of the shock very small at relevant
scales. For simplicity, many models of rotating detonation, including the one presented in this
thesis, also neglect the width of the combustion region after the shock, compressing the ZND
model down to a 0D jump condition. The combustion region, as predicted by the ZND model, is
18

typically on the order of 1 millimeter for stoichiometric H2-Air, making this a reasonable
assumption for most combustors at the current scale of modern turbine engines.

only on the temperature, pressure, and chemical composition of the reactants. Figure 3 shows the

to both reactant pressure and temperature, with product pressure increasing with increasing

2.2

Existing RDC Models Based on the MOC

There are two rotating detonation models which use the method of characteristics which
have been previously published. The first, published in 2017 by Jorge Sousa, James Braun, and
Guillermo Paniagua of Purdue University [11], is the simpler of the two and serves as the basis for
the model described in this thesis. The second was developed by Robert Fievisohn and Kenneth
Yu of the University of Maryland around the same time [12]. All of the models share some
similarities. The detonation is treated as a 0D jump condition solved using the ZND model,
although the flow entering the detonation may or may not be assumed uniform. All of the models
27

use the 2D MOC to solve the flow properties in the region behind the detonation in the frame of
reference of the detonation wave. The oblique shock attached to the detonation is treated using the
Rankine Hugoniot equations. Additionally, all of the models require iterations to converge to a
physical solution. Since the shape and size of the refilling region is not known a priori, some
physical parameter – usually wave height – must be guessed upon each iteration until the value
converges. Similarly, the angle of the oblique shock must be guessed before solving the MOC and
then updated after solving until its value converges.

2.2.1

The Model of Sousa et al.
The model of Sousa et al. [11] fixes the height of the wave as an input, using the reactant

injection velocity as the parameter which determines the size of the refill region. The other inputs
are the stagnation pressure and temperature of the premixed reactants. The entire refill region is
assumed to be uniform, allowing the ZND model to be solved only once per iteration of the refill
region. By guessing the reactant injection velocity, the refill region can be fixed and the properties
after the detonation can be found using the ZND model. The angle of the oblique shock is guessed,
fixing the remaining flow structures which bound the three flow regions. Every region in the
domain is bounded by straight lines, whereas in a real combustor the slip lines, detonation, and
oblique shock may all be curved. The Irrotational MOC is initialized with points spaced evenly on
the detonation line. After solving the post-detonation region with the MOC, the injection velocity
of the reactants is updated and the solving process repeated until the guesses for the injection
velocity and oblique shock angle are converged. The updated guess for reactant injection velocity
is found by finding the point on the inlet wall where the pressure after the detonation is less than
the total pressure of the reactants, which is where injection can begin. Once converged, the Rankine
Hugoniot relations are used to cross the infinitesimally thin oblique shock. After the oblique shock,
the properties are assumed to be constant along straight streamlines. The simplicity of this model
reduces its computation time to just 200 seconds compared to the hours or days reported for 2D
URANS. The results of Sousa et al. show that total pressure gain is sensitive to reactant
temperature, and that there is a peak pressure gain for a specific wave height, which they postulate
to be the steady state solution with lowest entropy production.

28

2.2.2

The Model of Fievisohn and Yu
The model of Fievisohn and Yu [12] makes fewer assumptions than the model of Sousa et

al. and makes greater use of iteration. The reactant stagnation pressure and temperature are the
primary inputs. The model starts by essentially guessing the entire solution. The pressure and
temperature directly ahead of the detonation are guessed, as well as the detonation inclination
angle and height. The conditions ahead of the oblique shock are assumed to be that of isentropically
expanded products from the detonation, and the angle of the oblique shock must be guessed. Then
the solving procedure for the post-detonation and post-oblique regions may begin. The in the postdetonation region is initialized using the expansion fans centered at the ends of the detonation line
as characteristic. Unlike Sousa et al., the slip lines between the post-detonation region and the postoblique and refill regions are not assumed to be straight. Instead, they are solved using an iterative
process to satisfy the equality of pressure and flow angle on both sides of every slip line boundary
intersection in the MOC, which solves concurrently on both sides. The refill region begins when
the static pressure in the post-detonation region is less than that of the reactant total pressure in the
plenum. Unlike Sousa et al., the refill region is not assumed uniform. Instead, the injection process
is modeled using a 0D model which handles both unchoked and choked injection of premixed
reactants. All three regions are therefore solved using the method of characteristics. Due to the
curved oblique shock and the nonuniform inlet boundary, the isentropic MOC is used instead of
the irrotational MOC. The compatibility equation of the isentropic MOC does not reduce to the
easily solvable form shown in Eqn. 2.12 and must be solved with iterations for each new point.
Once the entire domain has been solved, a new iteration begins using the same process with
updated guesses. Computation time has not been reported, although based on the number of
iterations required, it is expected that this model is slower than that of Sousa et al. The results of
Fievisohn and Yu’s model compare well with CFD solutions for values of thrust, 𝐼 , and fuel flow
rate, but the model does not accurately predict wave height. Another model, published by Robert
Fievisohn, John Hoke, and Fred Schauer, is an extension of the original model of Fievisohn and
Yu to quasi-2D. The MOC is derived with a source term for area change, which allows for
gradients of area to be modeled, with the exclusion of curvature and other 3D effects. Results from
this model are not well-documented [19].

Structure and Solution Process
The structure of the present model is based on the procedure laid out by Sousa et al [11].

Figure 11 shows the solution procedure.

Figure 11. RDC model solution procedure
The inputs to the model are the domain geometry specified by combustor radius and length; the
reactant state in terms of stagnation pressure, temperature, and equivalence ratio; and the Mach
number with which the premixed reactants enter the combustor. Given these inputs, all of the
reactant properties within the refill region can be determined using Cantera, an open-source
chemical kinetics module for MATLAB. The entire flow in the refill region is assumed uniform,
including just ahead of the detonation wave, allowing us to use the ZND model of detonation to
determine the post-detonation conditions outside of any iterative loops. This saves significantly on
computational time as the ZND solver takes several seconds to complete.

Once the MOC points have been interpolated onto the uniform solution grid, traditional CFD postprocessing techniques can be used, including the change of reference frame from the detonation to
the laboratory frame of reference. The MOC only solves the flow angle, 𝜃, and Mach number at
every point, so the pressure and temperature must also be found to fix the thermodynamic state.
For the refill region, the properties are uniform everywhere and known from the conditions
specified by the user. In the post-detonation region, the flow is assumed isentropic, and the total
pressure and temperature are known immediately after the detonation from the ZND model and
35

are uniform, which allows for the calculation of static pressure and temperature everywhere in the
region. For the post-oblique region, properties are known directly after the oblique shock, but are
not uniform. The model assumes constant total pressure and temperature along isentropic straight
streamlines in this region. Then these values are interpolated onto the MOC points to calculate the
static conditions. With the thermodynamic state fixed at every solution point, all properties can be
retrieved from the ideal gas equation of state or from the Cantera reference table lookup function.

Understanding how the Mach number of the reactants affects the solution requires a bit more indepth analysis. First, we start by analyzing the effect on the height of the wave. There are two
factors which affect the height of the detonation wave. First is the pressure ratio between the
reactants and the products immediately after the detonation. This pressure ratio determines when
the injection of new reactants can begin, and according to the ZND model is dependent almost

45

entirely on the reactant static temperature, as seen in Figure 4. For a fixed total temperature and
pressure of the reactants, increasing the Mach number decreases the static temperature and
pressure. As the reactant temperature decreases, this pressure ratio increases, blocking detonation
for longer and decreasing the wave height. However, the other factor affecting the height of the
wave works in the opposite way. This is the inclination of the detonation. The decrease in static
pressure and temperature of the reactants cause the velocity of the wave to decrease according to
the ZND model as shown in Figure 6. In addition, the velocity of the reactants increases with Mach
number. Since the wave speed decreases while reactant speed increases, the detonation becomes
more inclined as the angle 𝜁 increases according to Eqn. 2.21. The increased inclination of the
detonation, and perpendicular refill line, causes the height of the wave to increase. The two factors
affecting wave height, inclination of the detonation and the pressure ratio across the detonation
oppose each other. In
Table 6, we see that the increasing inclination dominates at low Mach numbers causing the wave
height to increase until it peaks and the effect of pressure ratio takes over.

the detonation wave. The increased inclination also means that the flow after the detonation is
directed more axially in the frame of reference of the detonation, causing the axial relative velocity,

This method for modeling multiple waves has its limitations. In deriving the model, we neglected
all curvature. In a real RDC, the detonation is not non-dimensional because the curvature changes
as we split the circumference into smaller wave periods. Therefore, as the number of waves
increases, the 3D effects become more important. In addition, the model assumes straight lines for
all flow structures, including the attached oblique shock. In reality, the attached oblique shock
does not extend infinitely far downstream of the detonation, which means that for very small
detonation waves inside long combustors this assumption is not appropriate. In addition, the model
makes no distinction between which wave number will actually occur.

Program Overview

The mixer design is part of a larger project to demonstrate continuous operation of a rotating
detonation engine. The engine will be composed of a cooled, H2-air rotating detonation combustor;
a mixer for conditioning the flow downstream of the combustor; and a commercial M250
helicopter engine donated by Rolls-Royce. The Rolls-Royce M250 architecture is shown in Figure
31. Air enters from the right side moving left, is compressed by a radial compressor, and is routed
externally to the leftmost side of the engine. Starting from the left side of the engine, the air, now
moving left to right, is heated in the combustor and enters the high-pressure turbine (HPT) before
entering the low-pressure turbine (LPT). After the LPT, the gases exhaust from the engine.

Figure 31. Original M250 Engine
In this configuration, the HPT drives the radial compressor, and the LPT drives the shaft of the
helicopter rotor through a mechanical gearbox. This engine will serve as the basis for the
continuously operating RDE, with the following modifications shown in Figure 32. The helicopter
rotor is replaced by a generator, which converts the rotational energy into electricity, which is then
53

dissipated in a resistor bank. The air compressed by the radial compressor is not routed into the
RDC, instead the air is exhausted through a valve or orifice plate. This allows the compressor to
dissipate the energy provided by the HPT, while being controllable by adjusting the valve or
switching the orifice plate. This will allow us to control the RDC and turbomachinery
independently.

Figure 32. Modified M250 Engine with RDC and Mixer
In the modified configuration, the original combustor will be replaced with an RDC and mixer.
The mixer will be placed downstream of the RDC and serves to condition the high-temperature,
unsteady exhaust of the RDC to be acceptable to the traditional turbomachinery of the M250. The
mixer will be connected to the turbine upstream of the first stage of the HPT, which is shown in
Figure 33.

Previous Literature

The design and investigation of components placed downstream of RDCs is an active area
of research. Much time and effort has been dedicated to numerically investigating turbines
downstream of RDCs. Liu et al. [20] numerically investigated a supersonic turbine downstream of
an RDC, while Liu et al. [21] investigated a subsonic turbine downstream of an RDC. Literature
on the passage between the RDC and the turbine is quite sparse, especially with regard to subsonic
passages. Braun et al. [22] investigated the flow damping across supersonic nozzles. Braun et al.
[23] numerically simulated accelerating, diffusing, and convergent-divergent passages
downstream of an RDC with subsonic and supersonic conditions downstream. They reported little
difference in exit fluctuation amplitudes between the diverging/diffusing passage and the
converging-diverging passage. Looking at the traditional design of diffusing passages without
RDCs, many sources agree that optimal diffusing angles to prevent separation are below 8-12 deg
[24] [25]. Above this divergence angle, separation leads to increased losses and non-uniform Mach
number. Higher divergence angles are preferred since they decrease diffuser length and therefore
weight and friction losses. Due to the presence of a choked throat, critical venturi design is also a
useful field to research. Brown et al. [26] compares several of the most common standards for
venturi design with varying inlet conditions. All of the investigated designs perform similarly, with
divergence angles in the diffusing section ranging from 3.5-6 deg. Particular emphasis is placed
on the design of the contracting passage upstream of the throat, which influences the location of
separation, although this was found to not be a concern in the design of the mixer.
On the experimental side, there is one particularly notable group that has investigated RDCs
with downstream turbomachinery. Naples [27] modified a T63 helicopter engine, similar to the
M250, with a rotating detonation combustor in an open-loop cycle. An ejector is used to add
dilution to the hot core flow. The ejector dilution used was up to 500% of the RDC exhaust and
was able to damp pressure oscillations by 60-70%. Temperature measurements before the NGV
indicated that the flow temperature was potentially within 100-150 K of equilibrium temperature
indicating that the flow was reasonably well mixed out, using a mixing region of just 17 cm. The
peak mass flow tested was 0.6 kg/s. In a later publication [28], the performance of the open-loop
RDE was compared to the T63 engine in open loop configuration with the stock engine. They
found that the exit of the RDC had approximately 25% unsteadiness in pressure compared to 6%
for the stock combustor and at much higher frequency. They reported similar performance overall

60

between the two configurations, appearing to demonstrate the feasibility of coupling an RDC to
conventional turbomachinery with minimal concern for flow fluctuations. However, the stock
combustor experienced issues with flame anchoring which eventually lead to considerable damage
to the engine, potentially explaining the similar performance to the RDC. In yet another paper [29],
using a similar T63 RDE configuration, dilution ratio was decreased to as low as 269% with total
mass flow rates as high as 1.12 kg/s. The turbine unsteadiness was measured to be 5-7x greater
than typical with turbine efficiency between 70-80%. Importantly, they found that the high
frequency oscillations decay rapidly in the turbine, leading to higher performance in later stages.

Manual Optimization of the Mixer
The area and shape of the throat were designed first. There are two competing ways to set

the area of the throat. The first was to set the throat area to choke at the design mass flow and total
pressure. This is achieved with an exit to throat area ratio of 3.03. However, given a choked throat
at sonic conditions, this also determines the Mach number of the exit, just upstream of the NGV.
Assuming the entire mixer is isentropic, the exit to throat area ratio that achieves the design exit
Mach number is also 3.03. When losses are considered, the actual Mach number at the exit of the
mixer will be higher than 0.21 which presents the potential for the flow to choke inside the NGV.
As a compromise, the exit to throat area ratio was set to 3.45, which decreases the total mass flow
to 1.90 kg/s and the exit Mach number to 0.20. This exit Mach number gives extra margin for
losses before the NGV chokes, while the decreased mass flow only serves to decrease the power
produced by the engine.

throat, and exit with small fillets at the throat. The bell shaped geometry has a quick opening after

Literature on Thermal Stresses
Thermal stresses may arise in a body absent external constraints when there are temperature

gradients within the body which produce different levels of contraction or expansion. An isotropic
body experiencing globally uniform temperature increase will not experience any thermal stresses
since all parts of the body expand or contract by the same amount. In essence, uniform heating
leads to uniform dilation of the body with no change in shape. Kent [30] covers in detail the simple
analytical models for thermal stresses produced by axial, radial, and circumferential temperature
gradients in an infinitely long, thin-walled cylinders. He notes that, in practice, the thermal stresses
are dependent on physical boundary constraints at the ends of the cylinder, however, these
analytical solutions are useful as a method to understand the sources of thermal stresses in general
cases. The analytical solution for a thin-walled cylinder with a constant temperature gradient in

Test Configurations
The experimental testing of the mixer will be done in two phases. First, a test of the RDC

and mixer together, without the M250, will be conducted. This test is intended to verify the
performance and safety of the mixer before integration with the rest of the engine. To ensure that
the full engine test conditions are replicated in this initial test, the back pressure on the mixer must
be reproduced somehow. The design back pressure on the mixer is 6.39 bar, significantly higher
than atmospheric conditions. Figure 55 demonstrates the necessity of adding an exit restriction to
apply this back pressure condition. This exit restriction is termed the “exit throat”, and will only
be used for the standalone test with the RDC. Without the exit throat, the diverging section after
the mixer throat acts as a nozzle and the Mach number in the instrumentation plane will be
supersonic (assuming isentropic flow). This will significantly alter the measured values and the
test condition. By adding a properly sized exit throat, the on-design test conditions can be
replicated.

3.4.4

Revised Mixer Design
In order to improve the safety factor of the part, the mixer CAD was re-designed with

transient thermal stresses in mind. The primary design strategy for reducing transient thermal
stresses was to make all parts which are exposed to high heat flux uniformly thin. As discussed
previously, decreasing the thickness of the parts reduces the radial gradient in temperature across
the thickness. Similarly, making the thickness of parts uniform, with no abrupt changes in
thickness, reduces the axial gradients in temperature which can cause thermal stresses if they are
not linear. The downstream flange of the shroud must be preserved since it will be needed to pass
instrumentation at the plane where flow temperature permits invasive techniques, and since it is
necessary to physically connect to the M250. In the region before the throat, the part undergoes a
relatively small transient change in temperature, with the steady state value being only 100 K
greater than the initial value. Additionally, since the temperature of this region is lower than near
the exit of the mixer, the yield strength is higher. Therefore, thick parts are acceptable near the
inlet of the mixer, but downstream of the throat they experience large thermal stresses during the
transient heating of the part. The steady state stress near the throat is caused by the axial
temperature gradient associated with the mixing of the flow streams, not by the geometry of the
part. This mixing is a necessary goal of the mixer; therefore, the temperature gradient cannot be
decreased without redesigning the aerodynamic shape of the mixer.

88

The revised design of the hub and shroud pieces are shown in Figure 68. The shroud has
been divided into two separate pieces. The inner piece forms the main flow path, while the outer
piece confines the secondary cooling which is injected into the M250. Recall that this secondary
cooling is not injected into the main flow and is instead used to cool the internals of the turbine.
By separating the downstream flange of the shroud from the part which is exposed to the primary
flow, the only piece that needs to withstand the transient thermal stresses is the thin inner piece of
the shroud. This piece is now thin along the length starting from the throat to the exit, meaning it
will experience smaller radial and axial gradients in temperature and thus smaller thermal stresses
during the transient. Similarly, the hub part has been consolidated into a single piece which is thin
after the throat. By making this a single part, the flange which connects the interchangeable throat
is removed. The thickness of the flange in the radial dimension causes it to experience large radial
gradients in temperature during the transient heating process leading to large stresses. Instead of
attaching the exit throat from the hub side in the standalone test, it will be re-designed and attached
from the shroud side downstream flange.

CONCLUSIONS

A model for a Rotating Detonation Combustor (RDC) based on the Method of Characteristics
(MOC) was developed. This model can solve a 2D unwrapped combustor flow field in under 10
seconds compared to the hours or days of a 2D URANS simulation. The fast model was compared
with a 2D URANS simulation and shows good agreement, although accuracy could be improved
through better modeling of the flow features. The model was used to perform a parametric study
based on reactant conditions. It was found that maximum pressure gain is achieved for low reactant
total temperature, while pressure gain is relatively insensitive to reactant total pressure. This result
can be understood as a direct effect of the ZND model of detonation. The axial height of the
detonation wave was found to increase with reactant total temperature and is also insensitive to
reactant total pressure. The height of the wave decreases with increasing equivalence ratio, while
pressure gain increases with equivalence. Varying the Mach number with which the reactants enter
the combustor produces interesting trends when reactant stagnation conditions are held constant.
Local minima and maxima were observed for pressure gain, wave height, and exit Mach number
which are explained by opposing influences of the changing detonation inclination and the
changing static conditions of the reactants. A methodology for modeling multiple co-rotating
detonation waves was proposed, which equates the number of waves to a nondimensional
geometric factor if combustor length divided by radius. Although this methodology does not

92

directly predict the number of waves which will form, it does allow for the comparison of
performance with different wave numbers. The main effect of increasing wave number is to
increase the exit Mach number, which is related to the decrease in individual wave height, which
is inversely proportional to the number of waves.
In addition to the RDC model, a subsonic mixer was developed to dilute and isolate the
exhaust flow of an experimental RDC from downstream turbomachinery. The channel cooled RDC
has a highly nonuniform exit flow with cold dilution air at 400 K near the walls and a hot core
flow of reaction products at 1800 K. The design of the mixer began with simplified models of
mixing and flow angle damping to provide turbomachinery downstream with acceptable flow
conditions. Two-dimensional RANS simulations were conducted of various geometries including
a dump plane configuration to determine the aerodynamic design with the lowest pressure loss and
most uniform exit profiles in temperature and Mach number. The dump plane configuration was
ruled out due to the inability to accommodate a choked throat to isolate the RDC from downstream
influences. The optimized mixer geometry is 10 inches in length and provides a nearly uniform
profile of temperature to the NGV of the turbine at design conditions. An initial mechanical design
for the mixer is evaluated using Finite Element Analysis (FEA), which discovered two primary
sources of thermal stress. The dominant form of stress during transient heating of the part is hoop
stress caused by temperature gradients in the radial direction. At steady state, the dominant stress
comes from the axial gradient in temperature as the cold flow near the wall mixes into the hot core
flow. These stresses were taken into consideration and a revised mechanical design was proposed
which significantly reduces the transient stresses during start-up by making use of thin walls. The
final part has a predicted minimum safety factor of 3.63 during the transient and 7.09 at steady
state, indicating that it can successfully handle continuous testing with an RDC.
